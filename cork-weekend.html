Cork City

Ireland's second city has a distinct personality -- scrappier and more self-assured than Dublin, with a food culture that rivals anywhere on the island. Cork doesn't try to impress you. It just is what it is, and what it is happens to be excellent.

Things to See

The English Market
Food market Since 1788
One of the oldest covered food markets in Europe, and Cork's beating heart. Browse artisan cheese, charcuterie, fish, bread, and produce from vendors whose families have held their stalls for generations. Eat lunch at the Farmgate Cafe on the mezzanine level -- it overlooks the market stalls and serves excellent food with good vegetarian options.
Mon--Sat, 8 AM -- 6 PM. CLOSED SUNDAYS and Bank Holidays.
Farmgate has vegetarian options
Shandon Bells & Tower
Church St Anne's, Shandon
Climb the tower of St Anne's Church and ring the bells yourself -- follow the numbered ropes and play a tune over the rooftops of Cork. One of the most unique things you can do in any Irish city. The views from the top reach across the whole city.
Mon--Sat 10 AM -- 5 PM, Sun 11:30 AM -- 4:30 PM. €5 per person.
Crawford Art Gallery -- closed until 2028
The Crawford Art Gallery is undergoing a major redevelopment and won't reopen until 2028. Skip it. Instead, walk the colorful streets between St Patrick's Street and the English Market -- they're worth exploring on their own.
University College Cork
Campus walk
The UCC campus sits along the River Lee, about a 15-minute walk from the city center. The limestone quad is genuinely beautiful, and the riverside walk through the grounds is peaceful. A good place to stretch your legs and breathe after a morning in the market.

Where to Eat in Cork City

Cafe Paradiso
Ireland's most celebrated vegetarian restaurant. Chef Denis Cotter has been doing inventive seasonal vegetarian cooking here since 1993 -- small plates, punchy flavors, beautiful presentation. 16 Lancaster Quay. This is the anniversary dinner. Book as far ahead as you can.

Market Lane
Restaurant Oliver Plunkett Street
An award-winning restaurant near the English Market, sourcing ingredients from the market stalls. Wide-ranging menu with strong vegetarian, vegan, and coeliac options. Relaxed atmosphere, regularly updated seasonal menu.

Farmgate Cafe
Cafe English Market mezzanine
Perched on the mezzanine level of the English Market, overlooking the stalls below. The food is sourced from the vendors downstairs. Excellent for lunch -- you're eating above the market itself, with the sounds and smells drifting up around you.
Mon--Sat, 8 AM -- 5 PM. Closed Sundays (market is closed).

Kinsale

A compact, colorful harbor town 25 minutes south of Cork. Kinsale is Ireland's self-proclaimed food capital, and it earns the title -- three Michelin-recognized restaurants and dozens more excellent spots, all in a town you can walk end to end in fifteen minutes. It's also the starting point of the Wild Atlantic Way.

The town's history runs deep. The Battle of Kinsale in 1601 was the decisive moment in the English conquest of Ireland. Spanish forces allied with the Gaelic chieftains O'Neill and O'Donnell landed here, but the combined Irish-Spanish force was routed by English troops under Mountjoy. It marked the end of the old Gaelic order and led to the Flight of the Earls -- the Irish chieftains fleeing to continental Europe, never to return. The Ireland that came after was shaped by that defeat.

Things to See

Charles Fort
17th-century star fort
A massive star-shaped fort overlooking Kinsale harbor, built in the 1670s. Walk the ramparts for panoramic views of the harbor and coastline. You can drive (5 minutes from town) or take the Scilly Walk along the harbor -- a gorgeous 35-minute waterfront walk with the fort growing larger ahead of you the whole way.
Open daily 10 AM -- 5 PM (March). Last admission 4:15 PM. €5 per person.

Your Weekend

Saturday and Sunday are yours. No long drives, no schedule to keep. You're based here. Below is a suggested shape for the two days -- but mix and match however you like. The only fixed constraint is the English Market's hours.

Saturday: Kinsale Day

Morning
Sleep in. You've been driving all week. Breakfast at your B&B or at The Lemon Leaf Cafe on Main Street -- homemade baked goods, good coffee, no rush.
Late Morning
Scilly Walk to Charles Fort -- a 35-minute waterfront walk from Kinsale town along the harbor. The fort at the end is a massive 17th-century star fort with panoramic views. €5 entry, allow 1--1.5 hours for the fort and the walk back.
Lunch
Fishy Fishy on Crowley's Quay. Casual, colorful, reliably excellent. Good vegetarian options despite the name.
Afternoon
Wander Kinsale's colorful streets. Browse the galleries and shops. Walk along the harbor. Anniversary pace -- slow, together, unhurried. There's nowhere you need to be.
Evening
The Black Pig for wine and tapas-style sharing plates (great vegetarian options, intimate atmosphere), or go all-out at Bastion (Michelin star -- book ahead). Walk back to your B&B under the stars.

Sunday: Cork City Day

Morning
Drive to Cork City (25 min from Kinsale). Head straight to the English Market -- browse the stalls, soak in the atmosphere of a food market that's been running since 1788.
Late Morning
Farmgate Cafe lunch on the mezzanine, overlooking the market stalls below. Then on to Shandon Bells & Tower -- climb St Anne's Church tower and ring the bells yourself over Cork's rooftops. €5, genuinely one of a kind.
Afternoon
Walk along the River Lee through the UCC campus. The limestone quad is beautiful, and the riverside path is peaceful. Browse the colorful streets between St Patrick's Street and the market area.
Evening
Cafe Paradiso for your anniversary dinner. Ireland's most celebrated vegetarian restaurant. Not just "vegetarian food" -- genuinely one of the best restaurants in the country. This is THE meal of the trip.
• • •
English Market is CLOSED SUNDAYS
The suggested plan above puts Cork City on Sunday -- but the English Market is closed Sundays. If your Kinsale day lands on Saturday, swap the days: do Cork City (with the market) on Saturday, and Kinsale on Sunday. The Shandon Bells tower is open both days (Sun from 11:30 AM). Plan around the market, not the other way around.
Anniversary Dinner: Cafe Paradiso
Cafe Paradiso is the clear winner for your anniversary dinner. Fully vegetarian, fine dining level, and genuinely one of the best restaurants in Ireland regardless of category. Chef Denis Cotter has been doing this since 1993 -- it's not trendy, it's enduring. If you book one meal in advance for the whole trip, make it this one. 16 Lancaster Quay, Cork.

With Friends in Cork

You're not just tourists here. You're with friends who know the area, have a car, and can show you the Cork that visitors miss. One of your friends is pregnant, so the plan should favor comfortable places, good food, and things you can enjoy at a relaxed pace. No cliff-edge scrambles or 3-hour hikes.

Day Trips from Cork (with your friends' car)

Cobh
Harbor town 20 min from Cork
Last port of call for the Titanic, and a town that tells the story of Irish emigration better than anywhere else. The Cobh Heritage Centre covers the emigration story well -- €10, daily 9:30 AM -- 5 PM. St. Colman's Cathedral dominates the hill above the town. The colorful terraced houses cascading down to the waterfront are the postcard shot of Cork harbor. Walk the flat harbor path for views without breaking a sweat.
Pregnant-friendly: flat terrain, indoor museum, cafes nearby.
Midleton & the Jameson Experience
Distillery & market 25 min east of Cork
The Jameson Distillery runs guided tours -- about 75 minutes, ~€26 per person. Midleton Farmers' Market on Saturday mornings is one of Ireland's best: local producers, artisan cheese, baked goods, and hot food stalls. Sage Restaurant in Midleton has excellent vegetarian options and has won multiple awards. Note: you'll need a designated driver for the whiskey tasting -- your friends having a car makes this perfect.
Pregnant-friendly: walking tour, one person skips the tasting.
Kinsale
Food town 25 min south (if not already your base)
Charles Fort via the Scilly Walk is a 35-minute waterfront walk -- but note the walk back from the fort is uphill. Drive option available. The food scene speaks for itself: Fishy Fishy, Black Pig, Lemon Leaf. Desmond Castle houses the International Wine Museum. Compact town, everything walkable, lots of cafes for rest stops.
Pregnant-friendly: compact town, everything walkable, lots of cafes for rest stops.
West Cork: Clonakilty & Timoleague
Scenic drive ~45 min from Cork
Timoleague Friary -- 13th-century Franciscan ruins, free to visit, sitting on the estuary. Clonakilty has the Michael Collins House museum, independent shops, and Lettercollum cafe (vegetarian-friendly). Inchydoney Beach is a beautiful strand with an easy walk. Mostly a driving day with gentle stops along the way.
Pregnant-friendly: mostly driving with gentle stops.
Spike Island
Island fortress Ferry from Cobh, 15 min crossing
Ireland's answer to Alcatraz. A historic island fortress used from the 7th century through to 2004 -- monks, rebels, convicts, soldiers. Guided tours run about 2 hours. €18/adult, ferry included. Check March hours -- they may have a limited winter schedule.
Note: involves walking on uneven surfaces and stairs. Ask about accessibility before booking.
Fota Wildlife Park
Wildlife park 15 min from Cork, near Cobh
An open-plan wildlife park where animals roam freely -- cheetahs, penguins, lemurs, red pandas. Flat paths throughout, accessible, benches everywhere. Indoor cafe and picnic areas. €18.50/adult, open daily from 10 AM. One of the best day-out options if you want something easy and enjoyable without thinking too hard about it.
Pregnant-friendly: excellent. Flat easy walking, indoor cafe, rest spots throughout.

Low-Key Cork Activities

For when you don't want to leave the city:

Fitzgerald Park & Cork Public Museum
Park Free museum
A riverside park with a free museum inside it. Gentle walk, green space, somewhere to sit and talk. Perfect for a pregnant friend -- benches, flat paths, and a cafe nearby.
The Marina
Riverside walk East of city centre
A flat riverside walk popular with locals. No hills, no scrambling, just a long straight path along the water. Go as far as you like and turn back.
Nano Nagle Place
Heritage centre Cork's medieval heart
A heritage centre and gardens tucked into Cork's oldest quarter. Peaceful walled gardens, a good cafe, and the story of Nano Nagle -- an 18th-century woman who defied the Penal Laws to educate the poor. Accessible and calm.
Shopping on Oliver Plunkett Street
Independent shops
Independent shops, no chains. Browse at your own pace. Cork's main shopping street has more character than most -- ducking into side lanes is half the fun.
Triskel Arts Centre
Arts venue Converted church
Cinema, gallery, and performance space housed in a converted church. Check what's on -- it could be a film, an exhibition, or live music. A good rainy-day option with seats and atmosphere.
