If You Have a Round-Trip Car

Monday: The drive north

Leave Cork Monday morning or early afternoon. The route is M8/M7 motorway the whole way: 265 km, about 3h05 in ideal conditions. Budget 3h23--3h33 realistic, accounting for fuel stops and the usual motorway slowdowns around Portlaoise.

The drive itself is fast and straight -- no narrow coastal roads, no single-track boreens. Just motorway. The real question is whether you stop along the way.

En-route stops worth making

Rock of Cashel
Medieval Cashel, Co. Tipperary — ~1.5 hrs from Cork
One of Ireland's most iconic silhouettes. A cluster of 12th-century ruins on a dramatic hilltop -- cathedral, round tower, Romanesque chapel, all of it exposed to the wind on a limestone outcrop rising from the Tipperary plain. This was the seat of the Kings of Munster for centuries. It's right off the M8. You can see it from the motorway.
Open 9:00 AM -- 5:00 PM in March — EUR 8 — allow 45--60 min
Cahir Castle
Medieval Cahir, Co. Tipperary — ~20 min north of Cashel
One of the largest and best-preserved medieval castles in Ireland, sitting on a rock island in the River Suir. Cromwell besieged it in 1599 and barely dented it. If you're already stopping at Cashel, Cahir is a quick add-on just up the road.
Open daily — EUR 5 — allow ~45 min
Kilkenny
Medieval Food Shopping Co. Kilkenny — ~1.5 hrs from Dublin Airport
The Medieval Mile connects Kilkenny Castle to St. Canice's Cathedral through narrow lanes lined with craft shops, pubs, and the Smithwick's brewery. It's one of the most walkable towns in Ireland. If you overnight here, it's only 1h30--1h45 to the airport Tuesday morning.
Kilkenny Castle: open daily, EUR 8 — St. Canice's Cathedral: EUR 6
Picking your stops
  • Cashel + Cahir are a natural pair -- 20 minutes apart, both off the M8. Together they add about 2 hours to the drive.
  • Kilkenny works best as an overnight. It's slightly off the M8 (you'd take the M9 fork), but it's a better town to spend time in than anywhere else on this route.
  • All three is doable if you leave Cork by 10 AM. Cashel and Cahir in the early afternoon, then on to Kilkenny by late afternoon.

Dublin Monday Evening

If you make it to Dublin on Monday, you get a proper farewell night. The weather's cooperating -- 10/7°C, partly cloudy, barely any rain. A good evening for walking.

Dinner

These aren't all vegetarian restaurants -- they're places with genuinely good vegetarian options. Savannah won't be stuck with a side salad at any of them.

Glas Restaurant
Fine Dining Chatham Street, Dublin 2
100% plant-based, 5-course seasonal tasting menu. Won "Best Sustainable Practices" in Ireland. The kind of meal you'll remember. This is the move if you both want to eat plant-based on your last night.
Reservations recommended via glasrestaurant.ie

After dinner

Palace Bar
Whiskey 21 Fleet Street
Over 100 Irish whiskeys. One of Dublin's oldest pubs (1823), tucked on Fleet Street just off Temple Bar. Quieter and more authentic than the tourist pubs. Dark wood, stained glass, no TV screens. Order something you can't get at home.
Temple Bar Area
Live Music
Yes, it's touristy. But on a Monday night it's less hectic, and you'll still find genuine trad sessions in the smaller pubs. Walk through, pop into wherever sounds good.
Walk Along the Liffey
Romantic
Ha'penny Bridge lit up at night, the Custom House, the Samuel Beckett Bridge. Dublin's riverfront is at its most atmospheric after dark. A quiet walk to close out the trip.

Monday afternoon in Dublin (if arriving early)

Glasnevin Cemetery
History Finglas Road
The resting place of Michael Collins, de Valera, and Countess Markievicz. Free to walk the grounds. A sober, powerful place.
Collins Barracks — National Museum
History Benburb Street — FREE
"Proclaiming a Republic" exhibition traces the 1916 Rising and independence movement. Quieter than Kilmainham Gaol, completely free. Worth an hour or two.
GPO & Witness History Museum
1916 Rising O'Connell Street
The General Post Office was rebel headquarters during the Easter Rising. The bullet marks are still in the columns. The Witness History exhibition inside is well done and interactive.
Kevin & Howlin
Shopping 31 Nassau Street
Ireland's most respected tweed shop. Donegal tweed jackets, caps, scarves -- the real thing, not tourist tat. If you didn't make it here on Day 1, Monday afternoon is your final window.
Closes 5:30 PM
EPIC The Irish Emigration Museum
Interactive CHQ Building, Custom House Quay — ~EUR 17
Interactive museum tracing the Irish diaspora. Near the docks, well-designed, and less crowded than the bigger attractions.
Last Chance
This is your final window for Kilmainham Gaol if you haven't been. Check for cancellation tickets at kilmainhamgaolmuseum.ie -- Monday afternoon tours sometimes have openings from day-of cancellations. It's the single most powerful political history site in Ireland, and it's gone once you leave.

If You Dropped the Car in Cork (One-Way)

Monday: Your bonus day

This is the gift of the one-way rental. No driving. No schedule. No motorways. Monday is yours.

Sleep in. Have breakfast at your B&B or hotel. Take your time.

Morning and afternoon in Cork

If you didn't go to the English Market on Sunday -- or even if you did -- go again. It's that good. Wander the stalls, have lunch at the Farmgate Cafe upstairs (looking down over the market floor), and take your time about it.

In the afternoon, walk along the River Lee. Have one last coffee. Buy something you'll remember. Cork is a city that rewards slow days, and this is the one day you don't have to rush through it.

Getting to Dublin: Irish Rail

Cork Kent Station to Dublin Heuston
Trains run roughly hourly. Journey time: 2h15--2h30. Fares: EUR 25--40 per person. Book at irishrail.ie or buy at the station. The train itself is pleasant -- comfortable seats, wifi, scenery through the midlands. From Dublin Heuston: taxi to city centre (EUR 15--20) or taxi to airport area (EUR 30--40).

Take a late afternoon or evening train. You'll arrive in Dublin with time for dinner and an evening walk. Everything in the "Dublin Monday Evening" section above applies to you too.

Tuesday — Departure Day

Flight: EI 0107, 16:45 DUB → 20:25 JFK, Terminal 2. Booking reference: 32B7CB.

Weather Warning
Tuesday's forecast is RAIN -- 14mm in Dublin. The worst weather day of the trip. Wind 5--9 m/s (11--20 mph). Allow extra time if driving. Visibility will be poor, roads will be wet, and you'll want windshield wipers you trust.

US Preclearance: why you need extra time

Dublin has US Preclearance. You clear US customs and immigration before you board. That means when you land at JFK, you walk straight out like a domestic arrival. No immigration queue, no customs line. It's wonderful on the JFK end, but it adds 30--60 minutes at Dublin Airport. Budget accordingly.

Car return process

Allow 30 minutes from pulling into the rental village to stepping into the terminal.

  • Fill petrol at station near rental village (on the approach road, follow signs)
  • Check car for damage -- walk around it, photograph all four sides, roof, and wheels
  • Drive to rental company's return bay
  • Hand over keys, get return receipt
  • Take shuttle bus to Terminal 2 (runs every 5--10 minutes)
M50 Toll
The M50 (Dublin's ring road) has a camera-based toll. No stopping, no toll booth -- it photographs your plate. EUR 3.80 for rental cars, auto-charged by the rental company. Nothing you need to do.

Silver Lining
You've had six days of mostly dry weather. Cloud, yes. Mist, sure. But the rain held off for the Aran Islands, for the Cliffs of Moher, for every drive along the coast. Tuesday's downpour is Ireland's parting gift -- a reminder that you got incredibly lucky with the weather, and a reason not to feel bad about leaving.
At JFK
US Preclearance means you walk off the plane and straight out of the airport. No immigration line, no customs hall, no declaration forms. Grab your checked bags if you have any, and you're in New York. Fifteen minutes from wheels down to outside. Welcome home.
