Good news: you're getting a solid week. The only real rain day is Tuesday -- and you'll be heading to the airport. Wind is constant (this is the Atlantic coast), and the west is typically 1--3°C cooler than Dublin. The strategy is simple: layers plus waterproof shell.

Ireland's weather changes fast
You can get sun, rain, and wind in the same hour. Don't plan around "rain days" vs. "dry days" -- plan around having the right gear at all times. A waterproof outer layer plus layers you can add or remove is the entire strategy.

What to Pack

Layers -- the key strategy

Ireland in March doesn't get properly cold, but it doesn't get warm either. The temperature hovers in a narrow band (8--15°C) all week. The trick is layering so you can adapt as conditions shift throughout the day.

  • Base layer: long-sleeve tee or merino wool Worn under everything else. Merino regulates temperature and doesn't get clammy. A simple long-sleeve tee works too -- just avoid cotton if possible.
  • Mid layer: fleece or light sweater (2--3) The workhorse layer. A fleece or wool sweater over a base layer handles most of the day. Pack two or three so you can rotate.
  • Outer layer: waterproof/windproof jacket THE most important item you'll pack. Not water-resistant -- properly waterproof, with sealed seams. This blocks Atlantic wind, sheds horizontal drizzle, and turns any sweater into all the warmth you need. Get one with a hood.
Buy an Aran sweater on Inis Mor
The Aran Islands are the authentic origin of the Aran sweater. Prices on the island are good, the quality is the real deal, and it doubles as your mid-layer for the rest of the trip. One less thing to pack, one great souvenir to bring home.

Footwear

  • Waterproof walking shoes or boots Essential for cliff walks, island paths, and cobblestone streets. You'll walk through wet grass, mud, and damp stone. If your feet get cold and wet, the whole day is ruined. This is a non-negotiable item.
  • Second pair for dry days / city walking (optional) Nice to have but not essential. If you're tight on luggage space, the waterproof boots can do everything.
Aran Islands footing
The stone paths on Inis Mor are uneven, cracked, and often wet. No heels, no flip-flops, no smooth-soled shoes. You'll be walking and cycling on rough limestone. Proper grip matters.

Rain gear

  • Waterproof jacket (properly waterproof, not "water-resistant") Already listed under layers -- this is both your rain gear and your wind shell. One jacket, two jobs.
  • Small travel umbrella Useful in Dublin and Galway city. Completely useless on the Cliffs of Moher or Aran Islands -- the wind will destroy it. Leave it in the car on cliff days.
  • Rain pants (optional) Useful for cliff walks in drizzle. Not essential if you don't mind damp jeans, but a lightweight pair packs small and keeps you comfortable in sustained light rain.

Accessories

  • Warm hat and light gloves For island/cliff walks where the wind chill cuts through. Saturday's low is 3°C -- add 15 mph wind on an exposed cliff and you'll want these. Don't need to be heavy; a light beanie and thin gloves are enough.
  • Scarf or neck gaiter A buff you can pull over your face is ideal for the Aran Islands ferry and cliff edges. Wind on the west coast is persistent.
  • Sunglasses Yes, even in Ireland. When the sun breaks through the clouds, it can be sharp and low-angled in March. Also cuts the wind on exposed walks.

Practical items

  • Portable phone charger Google Maps navigation drains your battery fast, and you'll be using it all day while driving. A full charge backup is essential.
  • Car phone mount For navigation. You'll be driving unfamiliar roads on the left side. You need hands-free, eyes-up directions.
  • Travel adapter: Type G (UK-style 3-pin) Ireland uses the same plug as the UK -- three rectangular pins. This is NOT the standard European 2-pin plug. If you already have a European adapter, it won't work. Get a Type G specifically.
  • Reusable water bottle Irish tap water is safe and good. No need to buy bottled. Fill up at your accommodation each morning.

What NOT to pack

  • Heavy winter coat Temperatures are 8--15°C. A rain jacket over a sweater handles everything. A heavy coat is dead weight you won't use.
  • Formal clothes Ireland is casual, even at nice restaurants. Smart casual is the dressiest you'd ever need, and even that's optional. No one will be in a suit or dress.
  • Lots of cotton Cotton dries slowly when damp, and you'll be in damp air all week. Wool and synthetics are warmer, lighter, and dry faster. One cotton tee is fine for sleeping; don't hike in it.
  • Thermal underwear Overkill for these temperatures. A merino base layer handles the coldest mornings without the bulk.

The Bottom Line

Packing philosophy
You're not packing for cold weather. You're packing for changeable weather. A waterproof shell, two or three mid-layers, warm accessories for exposed walks, and waterproof footwear covers every scenario this week throws at you. When in doubt, leave it out -- you can always buy a sweater on Inis Mor.
