You chose the coast. Good call. The road from Galway into County Clare takes you through some of the most striking scenery in Ireland: the alien limestone of the Burren, the 214-metre sea cliffs at Moher, and the trad music village of Doolin. This page covers all of it, then splits into two onward routes at the end.

Two Ways South From Here
This page covers the Clare coast from Galway to Doolin. After Doolin, you'll continue south toward Cork for the weekend. Option A takes the full Wild Atlantic Way through Kerry (Dingle, Ring of Kerry) before reaching Cork. Option B shortcuts via Limerick to Cork in about 3 hours, giving you maximum weekend time in the city. Both are detailed at the bottom of this page.

Route Note
The Burren roads are narrow, winding, and slow. Don't trust Google Maps' time estimates here -- they assume you'll drive at the speed limit, which on a single-track road with stone walls on both sides, you won't. Add 10--15 minutes to any navigation estimate through the Burren interior.

The Burren

Nature Archaeology

Two hundred and fifty square kilometres of karst limestone that looks like another planet. The Burren is a geological anomaly -- bare grey rock stretching to the horizon, fissured and cracked, yet somehow hosting one of the richest concentrations of wildflowers in Europe. Arctic, Mediterranean, and alpine species grow side by side in the limestone crevices, a phenomenon found almost nowhere else on Earth. In March the early spring gentians may already be blooming blue in the cracks.

Cromwell's surveyor described this place in 1651 as having "not enough wood to hang a man, not enough water to drown him, and not enough earth to bury him." He wasn't wrong about the landscape. He was wrong about its value.

Poulnabrone Dolmen

Poulnabrone Dolmen
Neolithic Portal Tomb · R480, The Burren
A 5,000-year-old portal tomb standing alone on a bare limestone plateau. "Poulnabrone" translates from Irish as "the hole of sorrows." When archaeologists excavated the chamber, they found the remains of at least 33 people -- adults, children, and a newborn -- along with a polished stone axe, bone pendants, quartz crystals, and pottery shards. This was a communal burial site used over centuries, older than the pyramids at Giza.
Free. Open year-round, always accessible. Right off the R480, 8 km south of Ballyvaughan. Small car park beside the site. Allow 15--20 minutes.
Don't Skip This
Poulnabrone is free, always open, right off the road, and takes 15 minutes. You're standing next to something older than written history in a landscape that looks purpose-built for it. The scale of the limestone plateau makes it feel almost ceremonial. Wear sturdy shoes -- even the short path is uneven rock.

Burren Perfumery

The Burren Perfumery
Working Perfumery & Tea Room · Carron
The only perfumery in Ireland. Set deep in the Burren interior near the tiny village of Carron, it produces perfumes, skincare, and candles from Burren botanicals. The organic herb garden outside is worth a wander even if you don't buy anything. You can watch the production process and browse the full product range. The tea room opens for St. Patrick's Day (March 17); before that, a Winter Warming Room serves complimentary coffee and biscuits.
Free admission. Open 7 days, 10am--5pm. Tea room open from March 17 through October. Phone: 065 708 9102.
Worth the detour?
  • Yes if: You want a quiet break in the Burren interior, Savannah's interested in artisan skincare, or you just want to see more of the limestone landscape. Free admission, and the drive through Carron is beautiful.
  • Skip if: You're tight on time. It's about a 20-minute detour each way from the main route. If you have 4+ stops planned, this one might push things.

Cliffs of Moher

Nature Romantic

Two hundred and fourteen metres high. Eight kilometres long. The Cliffs of Moher are the most visited natural attraction in Ireland, and they've earned that status. The scale doesn't register from photos -- you have to stand at the edge, look straight down at the Atlantic hammering the cliff base, and feel the wind trying to push you back. On a clear day you can see the Aran Islands to the north and the mountains of Kerry to the south.

Cliffs of Moher
Sea Cliffs & Visitor Centre · Liscannor
The visitor centre is built into the hillside -- it's almost invisible from the cliff walk, which is a nice design touch. Inside: an interactive Atlantic Edge exhibition, two cafes, and a gift shop. O'Brien's Tower, built in 1835, sits at the highest point and offers 360-degree views. But the real experience is walking south from the visitor centre toward Hag's Head. That stretch is longer, less crowded, and arguably more dramatic than the main viewing platforms.
March hours: 8am--7pm daily. Adult admission €10 (online), €12 (gate). Children under 12 free. Includes parking, exhibition, and O'Brien's Tower access.

Local Tip
Walk south from the visitor centre toward Hag's Head for fewer people and more dramatic views. The main viewing platform near O'Brien's Tower gets crowded even in March. Allow 1.5--2 hours for the full experience. Book tickets online at cliffsofmoher.ie to save a couple euro and skip the gate queue.
Weather-Dependent
The cliffs are fully exposed to the Atlantic. In heavy rain or fog, you literally can't see anything -- the cliff edge vanishes into grey mist and you're paying €10 to stand in a cloud. Check conditions that morning before committing. The forecast for Saturday March 21 looks decent: 10/6°C, partly cloudy, minimal rain (0.1 mm), light wind at 2 m/s. Sunday March 22 is also workable: 10/5°C, cloudy but dry, moderate wind at 5 m/s. Either day is fine for cliff viewing. Dress in layers with a windproof shell regardless.

Doolin

Music Food Romantic

A tiny village at the edge of the Atlantic that somehow became the spiritual home of traditional Irish music. Doolin has three pubs, all within walking distance of each other, and all hosting live trad sessions most nights -- even in March. It's the gateway village to the Cliffs (15 minutes by car) and a good overnight stop before the route splits.

The Music Pubs

Doolin's three pubs are the reason musicians come here from all over the world. The sessions are informal, un-mic'd, and real. Fiddles, flutes, bodhrans, tin whistles, concertinas. You sit with a pint and listen. That's the whole thing, and it's perfect.

Gus O'Connor's Pub
Traditional Pub & Trad Sessions · Fisher Street
Established 1832. The most famous of Doolin's three music pubs. Nightly trad sessions from February through November. The sessions are open and un-amplified -- you're hearing the real thing, not a performance. Also serves lunch and dinner. Right next to the start of the Doolin cliff walk.
Open 364 days a year. Nightly sessions March--October. Sunday sessions run all year, starting early.
Vegetarian Options
McGann's Pub
Traditional Pub & Restaurant · Main Street, Roadford
Family-run since 1975. Nightly trad sessions in summer; regular sessions year-round. Cozy stone fireplace, vintage memorabilia, proper pub food -- stew with soda bread, fish and chips. The atmosphere here is slightly more intimate than Gus O'Connor's, which gets the tour bus crowd.
Phone: (065) 707 4133. Check Facebook for session schedule.
Vegetarian Options
Fitzpatrick's Bar (Fitz's Pub)
Pub & Music Venue · Hotel Doolin
Home of the "Wild Atlantic Session" -- live music every single night, 365 days a year, no exceptions. Inside Hotel Doolin, a 10-minute walk from Fisher Street. Polished panelling, natural stone, open fire. Over 100 whiskeys. Their own Dooliner Beer (Irish red ale) is worth trying. The vegan curry is a surprisingly good menu option.
Nightly music, 365 days/year. Food served daily.
Vegan Options Available
Three Pubs, One Night
All three pubs are within walking distance. The classic Doolin evening is to start at one, catch a set, then wander to the next. Sessions typically kick off around 9--9:30pm. Get dinner first, then pub-hop. You won't need a car.

Doolin Pier & Aran Islands Ferry

March Ferry Status
Doolin Pier has seasonal ferries to the Aran Islands, but the full schedule typically doesn't start until April. Doolin Ferry Company runs limited sailings from late February, but frequency is reduced (2 per day vs. 4 in summer). If you already visited the Aran Islands from Galway, skip this. If you didn't, check doolinferry.com or call +353 65 707 5555 to confirm March availability. The crossing to Inis Oirr (the smallest island) is only 15 minutes.

Where to Eat in Clare

The Doolin and North Clare area has a better food scene than you'd expect for its size. Here are three spots beyond the pubs.

Roadford House Restaurant
Restaurant · Doolin
Locally sourced ingredients, creative menu that changes with the seasons. The emphasis is on Clare produce -- seafood from Doolin pier, meat from local farms, vegetables from nearby gardens. Good vegetarian options alongside the fish and meat mains. Intimate dining room with a warm atmosphere.
Dinner service. Reservations recommended, especially weekends.
Good Vegetarian Options
Vaughan's Pub
Traditional Pub & Food · Kilfenora
In the village of Kilfenora, 15 minutes from Doolin. Traditional pub food in a great atmosphere -- Kilfenora is home to the Kilfenora Ceili Band, one of the oldest in Ireland. Vaughan's has been in the same family for generations. The barn out back hosts set dancing on weekends. Good for a lunch stop if you're driving through the Burren.
Food served daily. Check for weekend set dancing schedule.
Vegetarian Options
Wild Honey Inn
Michelin-Recognized Restaurant · Lisdoonvarna
The first pub in Ireland to receive a Michelin Bib Gourmand, and it's held the recognition since. Chef Aidan McGrath builds the menu around what's available locally that day. Creative, thoughtful cooking -- not fussy, not pretentious, just genuinely good. The vegetarian options are treated as seriously as the meat and fish dishes, not as afterthoughts. In the quirky spa town of Lisdoonvarna, 10 minutes from Doolin.
Dinner from 6pm. Closed Mondays and Tuesdays (confirm before planning). Reservations strongly recommended. wildhoneyinn.com
Excellent Vegetarian Options
Savannah's Diet
All three spots above have genuine vegetarian options, not just "remove the meat" placeholders. Wild Honey Inn is the strongest pick if you want a proper dinner out -- the chef treats veg dishes with the same attention as everything else. For a more casual evening, Roadford House in Doolin keeps things simple and locally sourced. Vaughan's in Kilfenora is the best lunch option if you're driving through the Burren.

After Doolin: How You Get to Cork

Cork over the weekend is the plan. The only question is how you get there. After a night in Doolin with the Cliffs and the Burren behind you, two paths head south. One takes the scenic route through Kerry. The other cuts straight to Cork via Limerick. Both get you where you need to be, but they're very different drives.

◆ DECISION POINT: SCENIC OR DIRECT TO CORK
Option A: The Full Route via Kerry
The Wild Atlantic Way south. Drive from Doolin through the Tarbert-Killimer car ferry, into the Dingle Peninsula, the Ring of Kerry, then east to Cork. You'll see the most dramatic coastline in Ireland, but the driving is long and you won't reach Cork until Saturday evening at the earliest.
227 km to Dingle OSRM: 3h 19m (realistic: 3h 39--3h 49)
Option B: Shortcut to Cork via Limerick
Skip Kerry, head straight for Cork. Doolin to Ennis (30 min), Ennis to Limerick (40 min), Limerick to Cork on the N20/M20 (~1h 50). About 3 hours total. You miss Dingle and the Ring of Kerry, but you arrive in Cork with the whole weekend ahead of you.
~3 hours total Maximum Cork weekend time

Option A: The Full Route via Kerry to Cork

Nature Romantic

If you take the scenic route, you're committing to the dramatic version of the trip. From Doolin, you'll drive south through Clare, cross the Shannon Estuary on the Tarbert-Killimer car ferry, and enter County Kerry. Through the Dingle Peninsula and the Ring of Kerry, then east to Cork for the weekend. It's long, it's beautiful, and it's worth it if you've got the time.

Long Drive Warning
Doolin to Dingle: 227 km, OSRM base time 3h 19m. With realistic road conditions, count on 3h 39--3h 49. Then it's another ~2 hours from Dingle area to Cork. The route goes via the Tarbert-Killimer car ferry, which saves roughly an hour compared to driving around the Shannon Estuary. Without the ferry, you're looking at 4.5+ hours just to Dingle. Plan the ferry into your day.

Tarbert-Killimer Car Ferry

Tarbert-Killimer Ferry
Car Ferry · Shannon Estuary Crossing
The Shannon Estuary is too wide to bridge at this point, so a car ferry connects Killimer (Clare side) to Tarbert (Kerry side). The crossing takes about 20 minutes and saves roughly an hour of driving versus going around through Limerick. You drive on, stay in the car or stand on deck, drive off the other side. Simple.
Runs every hour on the hour (roughly). ~€20 per car one-way. No reservation needed for most sailings, but arrive 15 min early in peak season. Year-round service.
Full Route via Kerry: What You Gain
  • The Dingle Peninsula -- Slea Head Drive, Dingle town, the westernmost point of Ireland
  • The Ring of Kerry (if time allows)
  • The full Wild Atlantic Way experience before arriving in Cork
  • Some of the most dramatic coastal scenery anywhere in Europe
Full Route via Kerry: What It Costs
  • Driving: 5--6 hours of driving across the day(s) to get through Kerry and into Cork
  • Cork time: You won't arrive in Cork until Saturday evening at the earliest, eating into your weekend
  • Fatigue: The roads in Kerry are narrow, winding, and beautiful but tiring to drive. After several days of this, you'll feel it
  • Risk: Any delay (weather, a broken-down car, lingering too long at a pub) and your Cork weekend shrinks

Option B: Shortcut to Cork via Limerick

Nature Food

If you take the shortcut, you've decided the Cliffs and the Burren were the coastal highlight, and now it's time to get to Cork. From Doolin, you head inland to Ennis, south through Limerick, and down the N20/M20 motorway straight into Cork city. About 3 hours of driving, most of it on fast roads. You'll be in Cork by Friday evening with the full weekend ahead.

The Route

Shortcut via Limerick: What You Gain
  • The Cliffs of Moher AND maximum Cork weekend time -- best of both worlds
  • Arrive in Cork Friday evening with the entire weekend free
  • No winding coastal roads -- fast, easy motorway driving
  • More energy for exploring Cork instead of spending it behind the wheel
  • Zero stress about timing
Shortcut via Limerick: What You Miss
  • The Dingle Peninsula -- many people say it's the single most beautiful part of Ireland
  • The Ring of Kerry and the Skellig Michael views
  • The sense of having done the full Wild Atlantic Way sweep
  • The Tarbert-Killimer ferry crossing (a small experience in itself)
When This Makes Sense
If it's Saturday and you haven't left Clare yet, this is the smart play. You'll be in Cork by early evening with Sunday and Monday still wide open. Kerry is incredible, but not if it means arriving in Cork exhausted on Sunday with half the weekend already gone.

Full Route vs. Shortcut: The Full Picture

◆ SIDE-BY-SIDE COMPARISON
Full Route via Kerry (then Cork)
  • Driving: 227 km from Doolin to Dingle alone, then ~2 more hours to Cork. 5--6 hours of driving spread across the day(s).
  • Scenery: The best coastal scenery Ireland has. Cliffs, Dingle, Ring of Kerry, then Cork.
  • Cork arrival: Saturday evening at the earliest. Maybe Sunday if you linger in Kerry.
  • Risk: Any delay and your Cork weekend shrinks. Weather, a breakdown, or a great pub could cost you a day.
  • Vibe: Epic. You'll tell stories about it. But you'll be tired when you reach Cork.
Shortcut via Limerick (direct to Cork)
  • Driving: ~3 hours, one shot, mostly motorway. Done.
  • Scenery: You've already seen the Burren and the Cliffs. The drive south is pleasant but not dramatic.
  • Cork arrival: Friday evening. Full weekend ahead.
  • Risk: None. You'll be in Cork with time to spare.
  • Vibe: Practical. Maximum Cork time instead of maximum road time.
Time-Sensitive: Count Your Days
If it's Friday and you're leaving Doolin: The full Kerry route is totally doable. You'll reach Cork Saturday evening with Sunday and Monday in the city.

If it's Saturday and you haven't left Clare: Take the shortcut via Limerick. 3 hours and you're in Cork with Sunday and Monday free. The Kerry detour would eat your whole weekend.

If it's Sunday: Shortcut. No question. Get to Cork and enjoy what's left of the weekend.
The Cliffs Are Weather-Dependent
Whichever route south you pick, check cliff conditions that morning before driving there. In heavy rain or thick fog you genuinely can't see anything. The money and time are wasted. If the weather is bad, consider waiting a day (if your schedule allows) or adjusting your plan.
If You Skipped the Cliffs Entirely
If you took Route C or D from the Big Fork and bypassed Clare altogether, you won't see the Cliffs of Moher. They're one of Ireland's defining landmarks -- 214 metres of vertical sea cliff, 8 kilometres long, the kind of thing that makes you understand why people have been coming to this island for thousands of years. It's a real tradeoff. But the midlands routes have their own rewards.
