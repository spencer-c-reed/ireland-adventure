However you got here -- straight from Dublin Airport or after a morning exploring the capital -- you're now in Galway. The city is compact and walkable. Most of what follows is within a 15-minute stroll of Eyre Square.

This is your afternoon and evening. Tomorrow morning you'll head for the Aran Islands, so tonight is about soaking up Galway itself: the shops, a distillery, the waterfront, and a proper dinner.

• • •

Afternoon Shopping

Galway has some of the best independent shops in Ireland for traditional Irish goods. These aren't tourist traps -- they're the places locals actually buy from.

O'Maille's
Shopping Traditional Irish Clothing
16 High Street, Galway City Centre. Hand-knit Aran sweaters, tweed caps, and traditional Irish knitwear. A proper locals' shop, not a tourist outlet. The staff know their wool and can help you find something that'll actually last decades.
Mon--Sat 9:30am--5:30pm
Standun
Shopping Hand-Knits & Tweeds
Spiddal village, about 20 minutes west of Galway on the coast road toward Rossaveel. Family-run since 1946 -- three generations. Huge selection of hand-knit Aran sweaters and tweeds at better prices than the city shops. Worth the detour, especially since Spiddal is on the way to the Rossaveel ferry port you'll use tomorrow.
Mon--Sat 9:30am--5:30pm, Sun 11am--5pm (seasonal)
Charlie Byrne's Bookshop
Shopping Independent Bookshop
Middle Street, Galway. One of Ireland's best independent bookshops. A labyrinth of rooms crammed floor to ceiling with new, secondhand, and rare books. Their Irish literature and history section is massive -- you could lose an hour here easily. Pick up something for the ferry ride tomorrow.
Mon--Sat 10am--6pm, Sun 12--6pm

Whiskey & Poitin

Micil Distillery
Whiskey Craft Distillery & Tour
Salthill, Galway -- above the Oslo Bar. A sixth-generation family operation that started as illicit poitin makers in Connemara and went legit. They distill poitin, gin, and whiskey using traditional methods. The tour covers the family history, the distilling process, and ends with a tasting. It's one of the more personal distillery experiences in Ireland -- small-batch, family-run, genuinely interesting.
Tours daily: 12pm, 2pm, 4pm, 6pm. About 75 minutes.
Pre-book at micildistillery.com
The 4pm tour works well if you shop in the afternoon and walk out to Salthill after.
Book Ahead
Pre-book your Micil Distillery tour online. They fill up, especially in season. Don't assume you can walk in.

While You're in Salthill

The Galway Famine Ship Memorial is a short walk along the Salthill promenade. It lists the names of over 100 emigrant ships that sailed from Galway during the Great Famine. A sobering, beautiful piece of public art right on the waterfront. Free to visit anytime.

Walk the prom itself -- Galway Bay stretches out to the west, and on a clear evening you can see the Aran Islands you'll visit tomorrow.

Cultural & Historical

Galway City Museum
History Free Museum
Spanish Parade, beside the Spanish Arch. Free admission. The "Revolution in Galway" exhibition covers Galway's role in the 1916 Rising and the War of Independence -- well done and worth 45 minutes. The building itself has good views over the River Corrib.
Tue--Sat 10am--5pm. Free.
Spanish Arch & Latin Quarter
History Walk
The Spanish Arch dates to 1584, built to protect merchant ships unloading wine and brandy from Spain. The Latin Quarter -- the tangle of streets around Quay Street and Shop Street -- is the heart of Galway's pub and restaurant scene. Good for an aimless wander before or after dinner.
The Claddagh
History Historic Neighbourhood
Just across the River Corrib from the Spanish Arch. This was once a self-governing fishing village -- the birthplace of the Claddagh ring (hands, heart, crown). The original thatched cottages are gone, replaced by housing in the 1930s, but the area still has a distinct feel. Thomas Dillon's on Quay Street has sold Claddagh rings since 1750 if you want the real thing.

Dinner

Galway punches well above its weight for food. All four of these are independent, locally owned, and have strong vegetarian options. Not vegetarian-only places -- just genuinely good restaurants where the veg dishes aren't afterthoughts.

Ard Bia at Nimmo's
Dinner Date Night Irish / Middle Eastern
Spanish Arch, Long Walk. Set in an 18th-century customs house overlooking the River Corrib. The menu leans seasonal and eclectic -- Irish base with Middle Eastern and North African touches. Vegetarian dishes change regularly but expect things like roast carrots with romesco and whipped feta, chickpea pancakes with spinach, wild mushroom tacos, and house dahl. The room is candlelit and atmospheric. A proper date-night spot.
Dinner: Tue--Sun 6--9pm. Closed Mondays.
Book ahead: Email ardbiagalway@gmail.com or call +353 91 561 114. Always full.
Strong Veg Options
Kai Restaurant
Dinner Modern Irish / Seasonal
22 Sea Road, Westend. Michelin Bib Gourmand and Green Star holder -- one of only three restaurants in Ireland with the Green Star, recognizing sustainable practices. The menu changes daily based on what's local and seasonal. Vegetarian and vegan dishes are always well represented, not token sides. Dinner is a three-course affair: starters ~EUR 11--16, mains ~EUR 29--40. The room is small and rustic-charming.
Dinner: Tue--Sat. Closed Sun and Mon. Reservations essential for dinner -- book via the website or call +353 91 526 003.
Strong Veg Options Michelin Green Star
Dela
Dinner Farm-to-Table Irish
51 Lower Dominick Street, Westend. "Seed to feed" philosophy -- they grow their own vegetables and bring them straight to the kitchen. Scandinavian-inflected modern Irish cooking. Reviewers consistently single out the vegetarian food as standout, not filler. Natural and organic wine list. The room is relaxed and unpretentious.
Dinner: Thu--Sat evening from 6pm. Brunch daily (walk-in). Email letseat@dela.ie for dinner reservations -- phone can be hard to reach.
Strong Veg Options Own Farm
Bierhaus
Casual Dinner Entirely Vegetarian Pub
2 Henry Street, Westend. Galway's original craft beer bar with a fully vegetarian kitchen -- 24 taps of local and international craft beer paired with comfort food done right. Smash burgers, the locally famous grilled cheese, spicebag quesadillas, veggie hotdogs. Not fine dining -- this is pub food where everything happens to be meat-free. Live music most evenings. Good fallback if you want something casual, or a second stop after dinner elsewhere.
Open 7 days. Kitchen serves daily. Walk-in, no reservations needed.
100% Vegetarian
Which dinner?
  • For a special night: Ard Bia at Nimmo's. The setting alone -- candlelit customs house over the river -- makes it the most romantic option.
  • For the best food, full stop: Kai. The Michelin recognition is earned. But it's closed Sun/Mon, and dinner is Thu--Sat only at Dela.
  • Wednesday night specifically: If you arrive Wed March 18, Ard Bia and Kai are both open. Dela only does dinner Thu--Sat, so save it for another night if you're here mid-week.
  • Casual / low-key: Bierhaus. Walk in, grab a craft beer and a burger, no fuss.

Where to Stay

You're taking the ferry to the Aran Islands tomorrow morning from Rossaveel (about 40 minutes west of the city). Stay in Galway tonight and leave by 9:30 AM to make the 10:30 ferry.

The Heron's Rest
Boutique B&B — City Centre
16 The Long Walk, right on the waterfront near the Spanish Arch. Small, charming boutique B&B. The owner, Sorcha Malloy, has turned a historic Long Walk building into a handful of clean, cozy rooms. Steps from Quay Street and Ard Bia. No car needed tonight -- walk everywhere.
Price: ~EUR 120--160/night depending on the room. Book direct on their website or Airbnb.
Avalon House B&B
B&B — City Centre
Steps from Eyre Square. Cozy rooms, free WiFi, free private parking (useful since you have a rental car). Rated highly for warmth and breakfast quality. 10% discount when booking direct with code AVALON.
Price: ~EUR 100--140/night. Book at avalonhouse.ie.
St Judes B&B
4-Star B&B — Lower Salthill
110 Lower Salthill. Close to the prom, Micil Distillery, and the Famine Memorial -- if you do the distillery in the afternoon, you could walk back to your room easily. Private parking. Short walk or quick bus to the city centre. Salthill location means a quieter night.
Price: ~EUR 100--130/night. Book at st-judes.com.
City centre vs. Salthill
  • City centre (Heron's Rest, Avalon House): Walk to dinner, pubs, shops. No driving after a whiskey tasting. More convenient for an evening out.
  • Salthill (St Judes): Right next to the distillery and prom. Quieter. Slightly closer to the road west to Rossaveel in the morning. But you'd need the car or a taxi for dinner in town.

Tomorrow Morning

◆ DECISION POINT: THE ARAN ISLANDS

The ferry to Inis Mor departs from Rossaveel at 10:30 AM. You'll need to leave Galway by about 9:30 to make it (40-minute drive, plus parking and check-in).

The real question is how long to stay.

1 Night on the Islands
Ferry out in the morning, hike to Dun Aengus (the cliffside fort), explore the island, stay overnight, return on the morning ferry the next day. More time for the southern coastal route afterward.
See the highlights Back in Galway by noon next day
2 Nights on the Islands
Same first day, but stay a second night. Rent bikes the second day, explore the quieter eastern end of the island, find the seal colony at Kilmurvey Beach, sit in the pub. A slower, deeper experience -- but it uses an extra day you could spend on the coast further south.
Deeper island immersion Burns one day from southern route
What's at stake
  • 1 night: You see Dun Aengus and get the island atmosphere. You're back on the mainland by midday and can push south toward the Cliffs of Moher or the Burren that same afternoon.
  • 2 nights: The island opens up. You'll bike roads with no cars, find beaches you have to yourself, hear Irish spoken in the pubs. But you lose a day that could go toward Kerry or Cork.
  • The honest answer: If you want a slower trip, do 2 nights. If you want to see more of the country, do 1.
Book Tonight
Book your island accommodation tonight. Inis Mor has limited beds, and places like Port Aran House fill quickly. Don't wait until you're on the ferry.
Ferry Parking
Leave your rental car at the Rossaveel car park when you take the ferry. It's an open lot at the pier, low theft risk, EUR 7/24hrs. You don't need (and can't easily use) a car on the island.
Good to Know
If you're driving to Rossaveel via Spiddal, stop at Standun on the way. Two birds, one stone -- you pass right through the village.
