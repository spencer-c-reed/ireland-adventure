Getting There

~9:00 AM — Leave Galway
Drive west on the R336 through the Gaeltacht. The road hugs the coast through Spiddal and into Connemara. About 37 km to Rossaveel.
~9:20 AM — Spiddal (optional stop)
Standun wool shop is right on the road. Family-run since 1946, it's one of the best places in Ireland for Aran knitwear, tweed, and Irish-made gifts. There's a cafe inside too. Open Mon–Sat 9:30–6:30, Sun 12–6. Stop here on the way if you haven't already.
~9:45 AM — Arrive Rossaveel
Leave the car at the pier car park (€7/24 hours). Check in at least 30 minutes before sailing. The ferry terminal is small and straightforward.
10:30 AM — Ferry departs
Aran Island Ferries from Rossaveel to Inis Mór. A 40-minute crossing. Sit on the right side for views of the Burren and the islands materializing out of the Atlantic.
~11:10 AM — Arrive Kilronan
You step off the ferry onto Inis Mór. The pier at Kilronan is the only village of any size. This is where the bike rental shops, pubs, and your accommodation will be.

On the Island

Rent bikes at the pier and you're free. The island is 12 km long with one main road and a web of stone-walled lanes. There are no traffic lights. Barely any cars. Just stone, grass, sky, and sea.

Bike rental
Aran Bike Hire — right at the end of the pier in Kilronan. €20/day for a standard bike, €40/day for an e-bike. Includes helmet, map, and roadside assistance if you get a flat. E-bikes are only available from this shop — book ahead, they sell out 60+ days in advance. Bikes due back by 6 PM.

Alternative: Pony traps (horse-drawn carts) wait at the pier too. More romantic, but you're on someone else's schedule and they won't take you to the Worm Hole.

Dún Aonghasa (Dun Aengus)

Nature History Romantic

The main event. A Bronze Age stone fort perched on the edge of an 87-metre cliff, with nothing between you and the Atlantic Ocean below. There are no barriers, no railings, no safety nets. You crawl to the edge on your belly and look straight down into the sea. It's been here for over 3,000 years.

The fort is about 4 km from Kilronan (20–30 minutes by bike, longer on foot). From the visitor centre, it's another 1 km walk uphill over rough limestone pavement to reach the fort itself. Wear proper shoes. Allow 2–3 hours for the full visit including the ride out and back.

Dún Aonghasa
Heritage Site — Bronze Age Cliff Fort
March hours: 9:30 AM – 4:00 PM (last admission earlier). Admission €5. Many overnight accommodations provide a free voucher.
The final approach is over bare karst limestone. Boots or sturdy walking shoes essential. The cliff edge is unfenced.
Open daily year-round (closed Dec 24–30)

The Seal Colony at Kilmurvey Beach

Nature Romantic

On the road to Dún Aonghasa, about 7 km from Kilronan, a colony of grey Atlantic seals hauls out onto the rocks near Kilmurvey Beach. At low tide you might see a dozen or more. At high tide, just their round heads bobbing in the water. Bring binoculars. There's a viewing point with picnic tables, and the mountains of Connemara fill the horizon across the water.

In Irish folklore, seals are selkies — creatures who shed their seal skin to walk on land as humans. The Oscar-nominated animated film Song of the Sea was based on this tradition.

Na Seacht dTeampaill (The Seven Churches)

History

About 3 km from Kilronan, these early Christian monastic ruins date to the 7th or 8th century. Despite the name, only two churches still stand — Teampall Brecan and Teampall an Phoill — but the site includes a beehive stone hut, a holy well, ancient Celtic crosses, and graves said to belong to saints. For centuries this was one of the largest monastic foundations in western Ireland and a major pilgrimage site.

Na Seacht dTeampaill
Early Christian Monastic Ruins
Open 24 hours. Free admission. No guided tours, but information panels explain the site. About 3 km from Kilronan — an easy cycle.

The Worm Hole (Poll na bPéist)

Nature

A nearly perfect rectangle carved into the limestone by the sea. It looks man-made but isn't. Water surges in through underground channels and crashes over the lip at high tide. The name means "hole of the serpent" in Irish — legend says a sea monster once emerged here to terrorize the island until Saint Enda killed it. Red Bull used it for their cliff diving world series.

It's about 1.6 km south of Dún Aonghasa along the cliff path (5 km from Kilronan). The path is marked with painted rocks. The coastline here is exposed and dangerous — don't swim, don't get close to the edge when waves are running.

Safety note
The entire western coast of the island is exposed to the open Atlantic. Strong, hidden currents around the Worm Hole make it extremely dangerous. Unexpected waves can overtop the pool without warning. Enjoy it from a safe distance.

Food on the Island

March reality check
Inis Mór in March is off-season. Many restaurants and cafes close for winter or keep very limited hours. There are only about 8 restaurants on the whole island even in peak season. Bring snacks. Pack sandwiches, fruit, chocolate. You don't want to be hungry on a cliff edge 4 km from the nearest kitchen.
Tí Joe Watty's
Pub & Seafood Restaurant
The island's best-known pub, right at the crossroads in Kilronan on the road to Dún Aonghasa. Locally caught seafood — lobster, crab claws, salmon, oysters. Coal fire on cold days. Warming soups and stews. No reservations — walk-in only. Voted top 10 pubs in Ireland for live music by Lonely Planet.
March status: Likely open but call ahead to confirm hours. May have reduced menu.
joewattys.ie
Veggie options available
Bayview Restaurant
Restaurant — Seafood & Irish
Right off the pier in Kilronan with views of the bay. Chef Byron Godoy blends Latin and Irish cooking. Sources seaweed from Blath na Mara, Aran Island goat cheese, organic produce from The Aran Artisan, and fish from local boats. Lunch and dinner menus daily.
March status: Unconfirmed for March — check their Facebook page or call before relying on it.
exploreinismor.com/places-to-eat-drink/bayview-restaurant
Veggie-friendly
The food plan
  • Bring snacks and water from Galway. Assume nothing will be open.
  • If Tí Joe Watty's is open, that's dinner sorted — seafood, stew, and a pint by the fire.
  • Your accommodation may offer breakfast (check when booking).
  • Some B&Bs will do a packed lunch if you ask nicely.

The Big Decision: 1 Night or 2?

◆ DECISION POINT: HOW LONG ON THE ISLAND
1 Night (Thu night, leave Fri morning)
Arrive Thursday, explore the island, stay one night. Take the 8:15 AM ferry Friday morning (Mon–Sat schedule). Back in Galway by 9:30 AM. Full day Friday to start heading south. You see the highlights, you get the sunset, you get the quiet evening — and you keep the rest of the trip open.
More of Ireland Fri AM departure Can do Cliffs of Moher AND Kerry
2 Nights (Thu + Fri nights, leave Sat morning)
Arrive Thursday. All of Friday is yours — no ferry to catch, no clock to watch. Cycle the whole island. Return to Dún Aonghasa in different light. Find the Worm Hole. Sit on a wall and do nothing. Leave on the 8:15 AM ferry Saturday morning. This is the deeper, slower, more romantic version.
Deeper experience Sat AM departure Lose a day of the southern route
What's at stake
  • 1 night: You get the island's best moments (Dún Aonghasa, the evening after day-trippers leave, the cliffs at sunset). You keep Friday fully open for the southern route and can potentially fit in both the Cliffs of Moher and the Kerry/Dingle Peninsula.
  • 2 nights: You get to actually live on the island instead of just visiting. Time to wander, time to sit in the pub, time to go back to a place that caught your eye. But you lose a full day from the south, which means tough choices ahead.
After the day-trippers leave
The island is at its most magical in the evening. The last ferry back to Rossaveel departs at 5:00 PM, and with it goes every tourist who didn't book a bed. By 6 PM the island is yours. Walk back up toward the cliffs at sunset. The light changes everything. This is why you stay overnight.
Opportunity cost
If you stay 2 nights, you probably can't do both the Cliffs of Moher and Kerry/Dingle on the southern route. You'll need to pick one or the other. If you stay 1 night, you can potentially do both. This is the tradeoff that shapes the entire back half of the trip.

A Day on the Island

However long you stay, here's how a day unfolds:

11:10 AM
Arrive Kilronan. Rent bikes. Drop bags at accommodation if check-in allows.
11:30 AM
Cycle west toward Dún Aonghasa. Stop at the seal colony near Kilmurvey Beach on the way.
12:00 PM – 2:00 PM
Dún Aonghasa. Walk up to the fort. Crawl to the cliff edge. Sit there as long as you want. This is not a place to rush.
2:00 PM – 3:00 PM
Walk south along the cliff path to the Worm Hole (1.6 km). Or cycle back toward the Seven Churches (detour on the way home).
3:00 PM – 4:00 PM
Cycle back to Kilronan. Snack break. Warm up.
Late afternoon
Check into accommodation. Rest. Read.
Sunset
Walk back up toward the cliffs. The light is different now. The island is empty.
Evening
Dinner at Tí Joe Watty's if it's open. Coal fire, seafood, a pint. Or cook in if you booked self-catering.
