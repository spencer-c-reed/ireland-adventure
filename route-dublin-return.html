Galway to Dublin via the Midlands

You're skipping the southern coast and heading back east. This isn't the "boring" route -- it's the route through the interior of the country, where the history is quieter and darker: famine, rebellion, and the slow erasure of a language. The M6 motorway covers Galway to Dublin in about 2.5 hours, but the stops along the way are what make it worthwhile.

Optional Stops Along the Way

Pearse's Cottage, Rosmuc

History Gaeltacht

Pearse's Cottage & Visitor Centre
Heritage Site — Rosmuc, Connemara (~45 min from Galway)
Patrick Pearse's summer cottage, where the leader of the 1916 Easter Rising lived to immerse himself in the Irish language before the rebellion. This is deep in the Connemara Gaeltacht -- one of the last places where Irish is still the daily spoken language.
The visitor centre opens March 17, so you'll catch it right at the start of the season. It's a small, contemplative place: the cottage itself, an exhibition on Pearse and the language revival, and the wild Connemara landscape all around.
Opens March 17 — allow ~1 hour for the visit
Note
This is a detour west from Galway, not on the road east. You'd visit this before leaving the Galway area, then double back to take the M6 toward Dublin.

National Famine Museum, Strokestown Park

History

National Famine Museum at Strokestown Park
Museum — Strokestown, Co. Roscommon (~2 hrs from Galway)
Housed in a Palladian mansion that was the site of one of Ireland's most infamous Famine-era events: the assassination of landlord Major Denis Mahon in 1847, after he forced 1,490 of his tenants to march to Dublin for deportation on coffin ships.
The archive here is extraordinary -- original landlord documents, tenant letters, eviction notices. It's one of the most powerful historical sites in the country, more affecting than most because it's so specific. These aren't abstract statistics; these are named people.
It's a 30-minute detour north off the M6 from Athlone. Allow 1.5--2 hours for the museum and grounds.
Open daily 10:00 AM -- 5:00 PM (confirm March hours: +353 71 963 3013 or strokestownpark.ie)
Verify before you go
The museum's standard hours are 10 AM -- 5 PM daily, but March is shoulder season and hours may be reduced. Call ahead at +353 71 963 3013 or check strokestownpark.ie the day before to confirm they're open.

Kilbeggan Distillery

Whiskey

Kilbeggan Distillery Experience
Distillery — Kilbeggan, Co. Westmeath (right off M6 junction 5)
Ireland's oldest licensed distillery, operating since 1757. If you missed it on the drive west, this is your chance. The 90-minute tour takes you through the original buildings -- waterwheel, copper pot stills, stone warehouses -- and ends with a tasting.
The real prize: their fill-your-own-bottle from a cask-strength single barrel. You pick a cask, they fill a bottle, you take home something you literally can't buy anywhere else.
Tour: ~90 min — EUR 35 per person
Timing the stops
  • All three stops would add 4--5 hours to a 2.5-hour drive. That's a full day. Only realistic if you leave Galway early morning.
  • Strokestown + Kilbeggan is the natural combo -- both are on or near the M6. Add about 3 hours total.
  • Pearse's Cottage is a separate detour west of Galway. Best paired with a morning in Connemara before heading east.
  • If you're heading to Dublin for an evening out, pick one or two stops, not all three.

Where to Stay in Dublin

You need one night in or near the city center. Here are three solid options in the EUR 120--180 range:

Blooms Hotel
Hotel — Temple Bar / Anglesea Street
Right next to Trinity College and Dublin Castle. Central to everything on your hit list. Temple Bar location means walkable to pubs, restaurants, and the GPO.
Check rates at blooms.ie -- expect ~EUR 140--170/night in late March.
Clayton Hotel Cardiff Lane
Hotel — Grand Canal Dock
4-star hotel in the Docklands area, a 15-minute walk to Temple Bar. Swimming pool and health club. Slightly quieter neighborhood but well-connected. Often has rates around EUR 130--160.
Dublin Skylon Hotel
Hotel — Drumcondra (north of city center)
About 15 minutes by bus from O'Connell Street, and closer to Glasnevin Cemetery. Drumcondra is a residential neighborhood -- quieter, cheaper, and on the road toward the airport for your Tuesday departure. Around EUR 130--165/night.
Booking tip
March 17 is St. Patrick's Day -- if your Dublin night falls on or near the 17th, prices spike 60--80%. Your trip starts March 18, so you're past the worst of it, but book ahead. A 13.5% VAT is included in quoted rates. Always pay in EUR, not USD, to avoid currency conversion fees.

Where to Eat in Dublin

Three dinner options with strong vegetarian menus:

Glas Restaurant
Plant-Based Fine Dining — Chatham Street, Dublin 2
Entirely plant-based, but this isn't a "salad bar" situation. Glas does a 5-course tasting menu with seasonal vegetables and local produce. Won "Best Sustainable Practices" at the Irish food awards. Creative, ambitious, genuinely excellent food.
This is the best option if you want a proper dinner-out experience where both of you eat well.
Dinner reservations recommended — glasrestaurant.ie
100% plant-based
Cornucopia
Vegetarian & Vegan — Wicklow Street
Dublin's longest-running vegetarian restaurant, open since 1986. Two floors, cafeteria-style service with generous hot dishes and salads. More casual than Glas, but the food is hearty and the portions are big. Open for breakfast, lunch, and dinner.
Good for a relaxed dinner without the formality of a tasting menu.
Vegetarian & Vegan
Brother Hubbard
Middle Eastern-Inspired — Capel Street
Middle Eastern-leaning menu with extensive vegetarian and vegan options. Capel Street is one of Dublin's best food streets -- lively, local, not touristy. The dinner menu features mezze platters, grain bowls, and seasonal mains that work for both vegetarians and omnivores.
Strong veggie options
