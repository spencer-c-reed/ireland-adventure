Cork to Dublin

You've had your Cork weekend. Kinsale, the harbour, the pubs, the food. Now it's time to point the car north. Cork to Dublin Airport is 265 km, about 3 hours on the M8/M7 motorway. The drive is fast and straight -- no narrow coastal roads, no single-track boreens. Just motorway.

The real question isn't the drive itself. It's when you leave and whether you stop along the way.

When to Leave Cork

Two strategies, both work. One gives you a Dublin evening, the other gives you a slow morning in Cork.

◆ CHOOSE YOUR DEPARTURE
Option A: Monday Morning/Afternoon to Dublin
Leave Cork by late morning, stop at Rock of Cashel or Kilkenny on the way, arrive in Dublin by evening. You get a full Dublin night -- pub, dinner, walk along the Liffey. Tuesday morning is completely relaxed.
Most relaxed Tuesday Dublin evening included
Option B: En-Route Overnight in Kilkenny
Drive to Kilkenny Monday afternoon (about 2 hours from Cork). Explore the Medieval Mile, have dinner in Kilkenny, sleep there. Tuesday morning it's only 1.5 hours to Dublin Airport. You get a historical stop AND an easy departure day.
Historical detour ~1.5 hr to airport Tuesday
Option C: Stay in Cork Monday Night
One more Cork evening. Leave Tuesday morning by 9:30 AM and drive straight to the airport. Tight but doable if traffic cooperates. Not recommended if you want any margin for error.
Extra Cork night Tight Tuesday Not recommended
On Option C
Your flight is at 4:45 PM and Dublin has US Preclearance, so you need to be at the airport by 1:30--2:00 PM. A 3-hour drive starting at 9:30 AM puts you there by 12:30 PM in theory, but any delay -- traffic around Dublin, a wrong turn, slow fuel stop -- eats into your buffer. Options A and B are safer.

Stops Along the M8

These are all on or near the motorway between Cork and Dublin. You don't have to pick any of them, but if you're driving Monday anyway, it's worth breaking up the trip.

Rock of Cashel

History

Rock of Cashel
Heritage Site — Cashel, Co. Tipperary (~1.5 hrs from Cork)
One of Ireland's most iconic silhouettes. A cluster of 12th-century ruins on a dramatic hilltop -- cathedral, round tower, Romanesque chapel, all of it exposed to the wind on a limestone outcrop rising from the Tipperary plain. This was the seat of the Kings of Munster for centuries before it became an ecclesiastical site.
It's right off the M8. You can see it from the motorway. Pull off, walk up, spend an hour, and get back on the road.
Open 9 AM -- 5 PM in March — EUR 8 — allow ~1 hour

Cahir Castle

History

Cahir Castle
Castle — Cahir, Co. Tipperary (~20 min north of Cashel)
One of the largest and best-preserved medieval castles in Ireland, sitting on a rock island in the River Suir. The curtain walls, the keep, the great hall -- it's all intact. Cromwell besieged it in 1599 and barely dented it. If you're already stopping at Cashel, Cahir is a quick add-on just up the road.
Open daily — EUR 5 — allow ~45 min

Kilkenny

History Food Shopping

Kilkenny
Medieval City — Co. Kilkenny (~1.5 hrs from Dublin Airport)
The Medieval Mile connects Kilkenny Castle to St. Canice's Cathedral through narrow lanes lined with craft shops, pubs, and the Smithwick's brewery. It's one of the most walkable towns in Ireland and feels more like a small European city than a county town.
This is the strongest overnight option if you're splitting the drive. Kilkenny to Dublin Airport is only about 1 hour 30 minutes on Tuesday morning, which gives you a completely stress-free departure day.
Kilkenny Castle: open daily, EUR 8 — St. Canice's Cathedral: EUR 6
Picking your stops
  • Cashel + Cahir are a natural pair -- 20 minutes apart, both off the M8. Together they add about 2 hours to the drive.
  • Kilkenny works best as an overnight. It's slightly off the M8 (you'd take the M9 fork), but it's a better town to spend time in than anywhere else on this route.
  • All three is doable if you leave Cork by 10 AM. Cashel and Cahir in the early afternoon, then on to Kilkenny by late afternoon.
  • If you just want to get to Dublin, skip everything and drive straight. Three hours, done.

Arriving in Dublin

If you drive straight to Dublin on Monday, you'll have a full evening and all of Tuesday morning before heading to the airport. Here's what's worth your time.

What to do in Dublin

The most powerful political history site in Ireland. Leaders of the 1916 Rising were executed here. Guided tours only -- book tickets NOW for tomorrow if any are available.

Ireland's best tweed shop. Donegal tweed jackets, caps, scarves -- the real thing, not tourist tat. On Nassau Street near Trinity College.

Fleet Street, Temple Bar area (but not a Temple Bar tourist trap). Over 100 Irish whiskeys. Dark wood, stained glass, no TV screens. A proper Dublin pub since 1823.

Inside the General Post Office on O'Connell Street -- the headquarters of the 1916 Rising. The bullet holes in the pillars outside are original.
History

History

Where to Eat in Dublin

Three dinner options with strong vegetarian menus:

Glas Restaurant
Plant-Based Fine Dining — Chatham Street, Dublin 2
Entirely plant-based, but this isn't a "salad bar" situation. Glas does a 5-course tasting menu with seasonal vegetables and local produce. Won "Best Sustainable Practices" at the Irish food awards. Creative, ambitious, genuinely excellent food.
This is the best option if you want a proper dinner-out experience where both of you eat well.
Dinner reservations recommended — glasrestaurant.ie
100% plant-based
Cornucopia
Vegetarian & Vegan — Wicklow Street
Dublin's longest-running vegetarian restaurant, open since 1986. Two floors, cafeteria-style service with generous hot dishes and salads. More casual than Glas, but the food is hearty and the portions are big. Open for breakfast, lunch, and dinner.
Good for a relaxed dinner without the formality of a tasting menu.
Vegetarian & Vegan
Brother Hubbard
Middle Eastern-Inspired — Capel Street
Middle Eastern-leaning menu with extensive vegetarian and vegan options. Capel Street is one of Dublin's best food streets -- lively, local, not touristy. The dinner menu features mezze platters, grain bowls, and seasonal mains that work for both vegetarians and omnivores.
Strong veggie options

Where to Stay in Dublin

You need one night in or near the city center. Three solid options in the EUR 120--180 range:

Blooms Hotel
Hotel — Temple Bar / Anglesea Street
Right next to Trinity College and Dublin Castle. Central to everything on your hit list. Temple Bar location means walkable to pubs, restaurants, and the GPO.
Check rates at blooms.ie -- expect ~EUR 140--170/night in late March.
Clayton Hotel Cardiff Lane
Hotel — Grand Canal Dock
4-star hotel in the Docklands area, a 15-minute walk to Temple Bar. Swimming pool and health club. Slightly quieter neighborhood but well-connected. Often has rates around EUR 130--160.
Dublin Skylon Hotel
Hotel — Drumcondra (north of city center)
About 15 minutes by bus from O'Connell Street, and closer to Glasnevin Cemetery. Drumcondra is a residential neighborhood -- quieter, cheaper, and on the road toward the airport for your Tuesday departure. Around EUR 130--165/night.
Booking tip
March 17 is St. Patrick's Day -- if your Dublin night falls on or near the 17th, prices spike 60--80%. Your trip starts March 18, so you're past the worst of it, but book ahead. A 13.5% VAT is included in quoted rates. Always pay in EUR, not USD, to avoid currency conversion fees.
