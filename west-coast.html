Wednesday: Dublin to Galway

You land at Dublin Airport at 4:20 AM. By 5:30 or 6:00, you'll have the rental car and be staring at the M6 motorway signs for Galway. This route trades the Kerry coast for the wild Atlantic side of Ireland -- Galway city, the Aran Islands, and the Cliffs of Moher before dropping south to Cork for the weekend.

4:20 AM Land in Dublin
208 km Dublin to Galway
2.5--3 hrs M6 Motorway
~€5.90 Toll each way
◆ FIRST CALL: WHAT DO YOU DO WITH WEDNESDAY?
Option A: Rush for the 10:30 AM Aran Islands Ferry
On the road by 5:30--6:00 AM. Drive straight to Rossaveel (past Galway, on the coast). Catch the 10:30 AM ferry to Inis Mór. You'll be on the island by 11:15 AM with a full day ahead. This only works if you don't linger.
Leave Dublin ~5:30--6:00 AM Arrive Rossaveel ~8:30--9:00 AM Ferry 10:30 AM
Option B: Explore Galway First, Islands Tomorrow
Drive to Galway at a human pace, maybe stop at Kilbeggan or Athenry on the way. Spend the afternoon and evening in Galway -- Latin Quarter, shops, a proper dinner. Take the ferry Thursday morning instead.
Arrive Galway ~9:00--10:00 AM Full day in the city Ferry Thursday AM
Option C: Skip the Islands Entirely
More time in Galway and Clare. Two nights in Galway, then the Burren and Cliffs of Moher at a leisurely pace before heading to Cork. Simpler logistics, no ferry schedules to work around.
No ferry booking needed More flexibility Miss the Aran Islands
The tradeoff
  • Rush for the ferry: Maximizes island time. You'll be exhausted from the red-eye, but the island itself is restorative. The risk is a delayed flight or slow car pickup killing the timeline.
  • Galway first: Saner pace. You get a proper Galway day AND the islands. The tradeoff is one fewer night on the island, or a tighter Friday schedule.
  • Skip islands: Simpler, but Inis Mór is genuinely special. You'd regret not going more than you'd regret being tired.

Optional stops on the M6

Kilbeggan Distillery
Distillery Kilbeggan, Co. Westmeath
The oldest licensed distillery in Ireland, operating since 1757. The "fill your own bottle" experience lets you draw whiskey straight from the cask -- €35 for the experience plus €35 for the bottle. It's a proper souvenir you can't get anywhere else. The distillery is right off the M6 at the halfway point to Galway.
Tours available; check kilbeggandistillery.ie for March hours
Athenry
Medieval town Co. Galway
A medieval walled town with intact 13th-century walls and a Norman castle. If the song "The Fields of Athenry" means anything to you (it's sung at every Irish sporting event), this is the place. A quick 20-minute walk around the walls is enough to justify the stop.
Driving on the left
The M6 is a proper motorway -- wide, well-signed, and forgiving for your first time driving on the left. Roundabouts go clockwise; yield to the right. You won't hit any narrow roads until you get west of Galway. Caffeine is not optional after a red-eye.

Galway city

If you're spending time in Galway -- whether a full day or a few hours before or after the islands -- this is what's worth your time. Galway is small enough to walk everywhere, big enough to surprise you. The Latin Quarter and the streets around Quay Street are the heart of it.

Latin Quarter & Spanish Arch
Historic quarter City centre
The medieval core of Galway. Narrow streets, buskers, colorful shopfronts. The Spanish Arch dates to 1584, when Galway traded with Spain. Walk from Eyre Square down Shop Street to the Spanish Arch and along the Long Walk by the harbor -- that's the essential Galway loop, about 30 minutes.
Galway City Museum
Museum Spanish Arch
Free admission, well-curated. Covers Galway from prehistoric times through the Claddagh fishing village to modern history. Small enough to see in 45 minutes, interesting enough to justify it.
Tue--Sat 10 AM -- 5 PM. Free.
Charlie Byrne's Bookshop
Bookshop Middle Street
One of the best independent bookshops in Ireland. Labyrinthine, overstuffed, and the kind of place where you walk in for five minutes and emerge an hour later with an armful of books you didn't know existed. Strong Irish literature section.
Standun
Clothing & gifts Spiddal, 20 min west of Galway
On the way to Rossaveel (the Aran ferry port). A family-run shop since 1946 with a massive selection of Aran knitwear, tweed, and Irish-made goods. Worth a stop if you're driving to the ferry anyway.

Micil Distillery
Distillery Salthill, Galway
A 6th-generation poitín-making family, now running a proper craft distillery in Salthill. Poitín (pronounced "potcheen") was Ireland's original spirit -- illegal for centuries, now legal and making a comeback. The 75-minute tour covers the history and includes tastings. Tours at 12, 2, 4, and 6 PM.
Tours 75 min, check micildistillery.com for booking

Thursday: The Aran Islands

This is the emotional heart of the trip. The Aran Islands are where the Atlantic meets 5,000 years of human habitation on a slab of limestone 12 kilometers long. No traffic lights. No chains. Irish is the first language. The landscape is so stark and beautiful it barely feels like Europe.
12 km Inis Mór length
~900 Residents
40 min Ferry crossing
Gaeltacht Irish-speaking

Getting there

Aran Island Ferries depart from Rossaveel (Ros an Mhíl), about 40 minutes west of Galway city. The ferry to Inis Mór takes 40 minutes. €34 return per person. Parking at Rossaveel is €7 per 24 hours.

Ferry schedule -- March
The 10:30 AM ferry runs daily year-round. The evening return is 6:00 PM (Saturday through Thursday). The early morning departure is 8:15 AM (Monday through Saturday). If you're staying overnight, you have flexibility. If it's a day trip, you need to plan around these times. Book at aranislandferries.com -- €34 return.

On Inis Mór

The island is best explored by bike. The roads are quiet, mostly flat with a few hills, and everything worth seeing is within cycling distance. You don't need a car here -- and you can't really use one effectively anyway.

Aran Bike Hire
Bike rental At the pier
Right at the ferry pier. €20/day for a standard bike, €40 for an e-bike. The e-bikes sell out 60+ days ahead in peak season -- worth booking ahead even in March. The island has some hills, and if the wind is against you, an e-bike turns a slog into a pleasure.
Book ahead at aranbikeire.com
Book the e-bikes now
E-bikes sell out early, even off-season. If you want them, book before you fly. €40/day is worth every cent when you're cycling into an Atlantic headwind.
Dún Aonghasa
Bronze Age cliff fort Cliffs
A semi-circular stone fort perched on an 87-meter cliff with a sheer drop to the Atlantic. No barriers, no railings -- just the ancient wall at your back and the ocean below. It's been here since around 1100 BC. You approach through a field of chevaux-de-frise (jagged limestone pillars set upright to slow attackers) and enter through a narrow passage. Allow 2--3 hours for the walk up, the fort, and time to sit on the edge and absorb what you're looking at.
€5 admission. 9:30 AM -- 4 PM in March.
No barriers at the cliffs
Dún Aonghasa has no safety railings. The cliff edge is real and unprotected. Don't approach the edge in high wind. Don't lie on your stomach to peek over (yes, people do this). Stay a body-length back and you'll be fine. The experience is extraordinary precisely because it hasn't been sanitized.
Kilmurvey Beach & Seal Colony
Beach Wildlife
A sheltered sandy beach at the base of the path to Dún Aonghasa. Grey seals haul out on the rocks here regularly. In March, the beach will be empty of tourists, which means the seals are more likely to be present and unbothered. Bring binoculars if you have them.
Na Seacht dTeampaill (The Seven Churches)
Monastic ruins Western end of island
A 7th--8th century monastic settlement -- despite the name, there are actually two churches and several domestic buildings. The site is atmospheric and usually deserted. Carved stone crosses, an ancient graveyard, and views across the limestone to the sea. Free.
Poll na bPéist (The Worm Hole)
Natural wonder
A perfectly rectangular natural pool cut into the limestone karst at the island's edge. The sea surges in and out through an underwater channel. It's used for the Red Bull Cliff Diving competition, which should tell you something about the setting. Beautiful and genuinely dangerous -- stay well back from the edge when the sea is rough. The walk from the road is about 20 minutes across open limestone pavement.
◆ ONE NIGHT OR TWO ON THE ISLAND?
Two Nights: Go Deeper
Stay Wednesday and Thursday nights. Two full days on the island -- time to cycle every road, watch the sunset from Dún Aonghasa, have a slow morning. The island reveals itself when you stop rushing. You'll lose a day on the mainland, but what you gain is harder to quantify.
Leave island Friday AM Tighter Friday schedule Deeper experience
One Night: See More of Ireland
Stay Thursday night (or even make it a day trip). More time for Galway, the Burren, Doolin, and the Cliffs of Moher. You'll see Dún Aonghasa and the highlights, then move on.
More mainland time Cliffs of Moher at leisure Less island immersion
The real question
  • Two nights: You'll remember the quiet. The stars at night (no light pollution). The sound of the wind. This is the kind of travel that changes your pace, not just your location.
  • One night: You'll see the big sights and have them in the memory bank. You'll also have more time for the Cliffs of Moher, Doolin's trad sessions, and a less-rushed drive to Cork.

Eating on the island

The island has limited dining options off-season. Some restaurants and cafes may be closed entirely in March. Bring snacks, fruit, and backup provisions from Galway. The self-catering accommodations below have kitchens -- use them.

The Burren

Between the Aran Islands ferry and the Cliffs of Moher, you'll drive through the Burren -- a lunar landscape of bare limestone karst that somehow supports an extraordinary variety of wildflowers (Arctic and Mediterranean species growing side by side, found nowhere else). In March, the early spring flowers are beginning to appear in the grikes (cracks) between the limestone pavements.

Poulnabrone Dolmen
5,000-year-old portal tomb Burren
A Neolithic portal tomb dating to around 3200 BC, standing alone in the Burren limestone. It's the most photographed megalithic monument in Ireland, and with good reason -- the stark dolmen against the empty karst landscape is haunting. Free, always accessible, right beside the R480.
Burren Perfumery
Artisan Carron, Co. Clare
A small perfumery making scents from Burren wildflowers. Free to visit, with a lovely organic garden and a tea room. It's a gentle stop between the stark landscape outside and the cliffs ahead. The perfumes make good gifts.
10 AM -- 5 PM. Free admission.

Cliffs of Moher

214 m Maximum height
8 km Cliff length
€10 Online ticket
8 AM--7 PM March hours

You've seen the photos. The reality is bigger. 214 meters of vertical cliff dropping into the Atlantic, stretching for 8 kilometers. On a clear day, you can see the Aran Islands you just left, the Twelve Bens of Connemara, and the mountains of Kerry to the south.

Beat the crowds
Walk south from the visitor centre toward Hag's Head. Most visitors cluster around O'Brien's Tower and the main viewing platforms. Head south and within 10 minutes you'll have the cliffs largely to yourself. The path is well-maintained and the views are better the further you go.
Book tickets online
€10 per person online at cliffsofmoher.ie. You can buy on the day, but online tickets guarantee entry and save time. The ticket includes parking and the visitor centre.

Doolin

A small village just north of the Cliffs of Moher, famous for traditional Irish music. Three pubs, all with nightly trad sessions. If you have time for lunch or an afternoon pint, Doolin is the place.

Gus O'Connor's Pub
Trad music Pub Est. 1832
The most famous of Doolin's three trad pubs, and one of the most famous pubs in Ireland. Trad sessions nightly. The building dates to 1832 and the atmosphere is everything you'd hope for. Food is decent pub fare.
Basic vegetarian options
McGann's Pub
Trad music Pub
Some locals prefer McGann's to Gus O'Connor's -- less touristy, more intimate. The music is often just as good. If one pub is packed, walk to the other.
Fitzpatrick's Bar
Trad music Pub
The third of Doolin's trio. Smaller and quieter, but the sessions can be brilliant. Three pubs in one tiny village -- the hardest part is choosing.

Getting to Cork

◆ ROUTE TO CORK: SHORTCUT OR SCENIC?
Option A: Limerick Shortcut (Recommended for Friday)
Doolin → Ennis → Limerick → Cork via M20. The fastest route south. Good roads, mostly motorway from Limerick. Gets you to Cork by early evening with time to settle in, find dinner, and relax.
~3 hours Doolin to Cork Mostly motorway Arrive by ~6 PM
Option B: Via Kerry & Tarbert-Killimer Ferry
Take the Tarbert-Killimer car ferry across the Shannon estuary, then drive through north Kerry. You could even detour through Dingle if you want the peninsula too. Beautiful, but adds significant time.
227 km to Kerry, then 2+ hrs to Cork Ferry + scenic roads 5+ hours total
Be honest about the time
  • Limerick shortcut: It's Friday. You've been going hard since a red-eye on Wednesday. Cork is the weekend destination, and arriving there relaxed and with daylight left is worth more than another scenic detour. Take the fast road.
  • Kerry detour: Only if you're leaving Doolin by noon and have serious energy left. The Shannon ferry is a lovely crossing, and Dingle is special. But if it's 3 PM and you're still in Doolin, don't even think about it.
Friday time check
If it's past 2:00 PM and you're leaving Doolin, take the Limerick shortcut. Full stop. The M20 gets you to Cork in about 3 hours. Trying to squeeze in the Kerry route on a Friday afternoon means arriving in Cork after dark, exhausted, with no time to enjoy the evening. Cork is where the trip shifts gears -- arrive with enough energy to appreciate it.

However you get there, the goal is the same: arrive in Cork on Friday afternoon or evening, settled and ready for the weekend. Cork is where you slow down, eat impossibly well, and celebrate your anniversary.
