Three days, one car, the wild Atlantic coast. You'll land in Dublin before dawn on Wednesday, drive straight west to Galway, spend a day on the Aran Islands, then push south through the Cliffs of Moher to Cork. It's a lot of ground, but the distances are manageable and every stop earns its place.

This page covers Wednesday through Friday -- arrival to the moment you roll into Cork for the weekend.

Wednesday, March 18: Dublin to Galway

Driving Whiskey History

Your flight lands at Dublin Airport at 4:20 AM. You'll clear immigration, collect bags, and pick up the rental car between 5:00 and 6:00 AM. Then it's straight onto the M6 motorway headed west. Ireland is still dark, the roads are empty, and Galway is waiting.

208 km Dublin to Galway
2.5--3 hr Drive Time
€5.90 Tolls (M4 + N6)

Driving Notes
Leave Dublin Airport by 6:00 AM, arrive Galway ~8:30--9:00 AM. The M6 is motorway the whole way. Two toll plazas: M4 near Enfield (€3.60) and N6 between Ballinasloe and Loughrea (€2.30). Cash or card at both. The midlands are flat green fields -- save your scenery appetite for what comes later. Drive on the left.

Optional stop: Kilbeggan Distillery

Kilbeggan Distillery Experience
Whiskey History Right off M6, Junction 5
Ireland's oldest licensed distillery, established 1757. About one hour from Dublin Airport. Shut down in 1957, revived and distilling again since 2007. The guided tour (1.5 hours, €35/person) walks you through original mash tuns, oak fermentation vats, and ancient pot stills, ending with a four-whiskey tasting.
Fill your own bottle: For an extra €35, fill a 70cl bottle directly from an aged cask using a valinch (whiskey-drawing tube) and attach a personalized label. This is unique -- nothing else like it on the route.
Open daily. First tours typically 9:30 AM. kilbegganwhiskey.com
Stop or push on?
  • If you stop: Budget 1.5--2 hours. You'll arrive in Galway around 11:00 AM instead of 9:00 AM. You definitely miss the morning ferry to the Aran Islands.
  • If you skip: Micil Distillery in Galway is your backup whiskey experience. But Kilbeggan's fill-your-own-bottle from the cask is one of a kind.
  • Recommendation: If catching the 10:30 AM ferry is your priority, skip Kilbeggan. If the ferry isn't in play today, stop here.

Optional stop: Athenry

Athenry
Medieval 30 min east of Galway, right on the M6
The best-preserved medieval walled town in Ireland. Athenry Castle (Anglo-Norman, ~1250) is open from March 13, daily 9:30 AM -- 6:00 PM, €5 admission. The Heritage Centre opens for the 2026 season on your arrival day, March 18.
The song: "The Fields of Athenry" -- the famous Famine ballad you'll hear in every pub this week -- is about this town. Written in the 1970s but set in the 1840s, about a man transported to Australia for stealing corn to feed his starving family.
A quick 30-minute walkabout covers the walls and castle exterior. Add 45 min if you go inside.

Wednesday Afternoon & Evening in Galway

However you got here -- straight from Dublin Airport or after a morning stop -- you're now in Galway. The city is compact and walkable. Most of what follows is within a 15-minute stroll of Eyre Square.

Shopping

O'Maille's (O Maille -- The Original House of Style)
Shopping 16 High Street, Galway
Founded 1938. Handknit Aran sweaters marked with each knitter's name, tweed caps, wool throws. Tailored costumes for the cast of The Quiet Man. One of the last places selling genuinely handknit Aran sweaters.
Heads up: The owners announced retirement in late 2025 after 86 years. Confirm they're still open -- check their Facebook page or call ahead.
If open: typically Mon--Sat, hours vary
Standun
Shopping Spiddal, Connemara (20 min west of Galway)
Family-run since 1946, three generations. The first shop to export Aran sweaters to the US. Huge selection of hand-knit knitwear, tweed, and Irish crafts. On the road to the Rossaveel ferry -- stop on your way to the islands.
Mon--Sat 9:30 AM -- 6:30 PM, Sun 12:00 -- 6:00 PM
Charlie Byrne's Bookshop
Shopping Middle Street, Galway
One of Ireland's best independent bookshops. A labyrinth of rooms crammed floor to ceiling with new, secondhand, and rare books. Their Irish literature and history section is massive. Pick up something for the ferry ride.
Mon--Sat 10 AM -- 6 PM, Sun 12 -- 6 PM

Whiskey & Poitin

Micil Distillery
Whiskey Salthill, Galway
A sixth-generation family operation that started as illicit poitin makers in Connemara and went legit. They distill poitin, gin, and whiskey using traditional methods. The tour covers family history, distilling process, and ends with a tasting. Small-batch, genuinely interesting.
Tours daily: 12 PM, 2 PM, 4 PM, 6 PM. About 75 minutes. Pre-book at micildistillery.com -- they fill up.
The 4 PM tour works well if you shop in the afternoon and walk out to Salthill after.

Culture & Walking

Galway City Museum
History Spanish Arch, Galway
Free admission. Modern building on the banks of the River Corrib overlooking the Spanish Arch. The "Revolution in Galway" exhibition covers the War of Independence and Civil War. Plan 45 min to 1.5 hours.
Tue--Sat 10:00 AM -- 5:00 PM. Free. (March 18 is a Wednesday -- you're good.)
Spanish Arch, Latin Quarter & The Long Walk
History Romantic
The medieval heart of Galway. The Spanish Arch dates to 1584, built to protect merchant ships unloading wine from Spain. Walk from Eyre Square down Shop Street to the Arch, then along the Long Walk on the harbor. In March it won't be wall-to-wall tourists -- you'll see the real city.
Galway Famine Ship Memorial
History Salthill Promenade
A short walk along the Salthill prom from the Micil Distillery. Lists the names of over 100 emigrant ships that sailed from Galway during the Great Famine. A sobering, beautiful piece of public art right on the waterfront. Free, always open.
Walk the prom itself -- Galway Bay stretches out to the west, and on a clear evening you can see the Aran Islands you'll visit tomorrow.

Thursday, March 19: Aran Islands

Nature History Romantic

This is the emotional heart of the trip. A Bronze Age fort on a cliff edge, Atlantic wind, stone walls running to the sea, a language older than English. If you do one thing in Ireland that makes you feel like you've left the modern world behind, it's this.

Don't waste Thursday
Thursday March 19 is the best weather day of the entire trip -- clear/partly cloudy, dry, up to 15°C in Galway. If there's one day to be outside all day, this is it. Plan your biggest outdoor activity here.
€34 Return Ferry
40 min Crossing
€20/€40 Bike / E-Bike
12 km Island Length

Getting there

~9:00 AM
Leave Galway. Drive west on R336 through the Gaeltacht, 37 km to Rossaveel. Stop at Standun in Spiddal on the way if you haven't already.
~9:45 AM

Key sights on Inis Mór

Dún Aonghasa (Dun Aengus)
Bronze Age Cliffs Romantic
A Bronze Age stone fort perched on the edge of an 87-metre cliff, with nothing between you and the Atlantic. No barriers, no railings. You crawl to the edge on your belly and look straight down at the sea. Over 3,000 years old. About 4 km from Kilronan (20--30 min by bike), then a 1 km walk uphill over rough limestone to the fort. Allow 2--3 hours total.
March hours: 9:30 AM -- 4:00 PM. Admission €5. Many accommodations provide a free voucher.
The Worm Hole (Poll na bPéist)
Nature
A nearly perfect rectangle carved into the limestone by the sea. Water surges in through underground channels and crashes over the lip at high tide. "Hole of the serpent" in Irish -- legend says a sea monster emerged here until Saint Enda killed it. Red Bull used it for cliff diving. About 1.6 km south of Dún Aonghasa along the cliff path.
Na Seacht dTeampaill (The Seven Churches)
7th Century
Early Christian monastic ruins dating to the 7th or 8th century. Two churches still stand, plus a beehive stone hut, holy well, ancient Celtic crosses, and graves said to belong to saints. About 3 km from Kilronan. Free, always open.
Seal Colony at Kilmurvey Beach
Nature Romantic
On the road to Dún Aonghasa, grey Atlantic seals haul out onto the rocks near the beach. At low tide you might see a dozen or more. Bring binoculars. The mountains of Connemara fill the horizon across the water. In Irish folklore, seals are selkies -- creatures who shed their seal skin to walk on land as humans.

The day-trippers leave on the last ferry, and suddenly the island belongs to you. Walk back up toward the cliffs at sunset. The light changes everything. This is the deeper, more romantic version -- but you'll need to leave on the 8:15 AM ferry Friday morning to keep the schedule south.

Take the 12:00 PM return ferry if you caught the early morning, or stay until the last return. Sleep in Galway, leave for the Cliffs of Moher first thing Friday morning. More flexible, and you keep Galway as your base.

The honest answer
  • Overnight: You get the sunset, the quiet evening, the pub by the fire after the tourists leave. The island at its most magical. But you must catch the 8:15 AM ferry Friday to stay on schedule for the Cliffs and the drive to Cork.
  • Day trip: You see the highlights and get back to Galway. Less romantic, more practical. Easier to manage the Friday drive south.

Friday, March 20: Cliffs of Moher & South to Cork

Nature Archaeology Music

This is a full day. You'll drive from Galway (or the Aran Islands ferry) through the Burren's alien limestone landscape, stand on the edge of the Cliffs of Moher, then push south through Limerick to Cork. It's the day that ties the west coast together and delivers you to your weekend.

Time management is everything today
Leave Galway by 9:00 AM to have time at the Burren, the Cliffs, and still arrive in Cork by evening. If you're coming from the Aran Islands on the 8:15 AM ferry, you'll be back in Galway by ~9:30 AM -- still workable, but tight. Every stop you add pushes Cork arrival later.
~1.5 hr Galway to Cliffs
214 m Cliff Height
€10 Cliffs Entry (Online)

The Burren

The road from Galway to the Cliffs passes through the Burren -- 250 square kilometres of karst limestone that looks like another planet. Bare grey rock stretching to the horizon, fissured and cracked, yet hosting one of the richest wildflower concentrations in Europe. Arctic, Mediterranean, and alpine species grow side by side in the limestone crevices. In March, early spring gentians may already be blooming blue in the cracks.

Poulnabrone Dolmen
Neolithic Nature R480, The Burren
A 5,000-year-old portal tomb standing alone on a bare limestone plateau. "Poulnabrone" means "the hole of sorrows" in Irish. Excavations found the remains of at least 33 people along with a polished stone axe, bone pendants, quartz crystals, and pottery. Older than the pyramids at Giza.
Free. Always open. Right off the R480, 8 km south of Ballyvaughan. Small car park. 5-minute walk from the road. Allow 15--20 minutes.
Don't skip this
Poulnabrone is free, always open, right off the road, and takes 15 minutes. You're standing next to something older than written history in a landscape that looks purpose-built for it. Wear sturdy shoes even for the short path.

Cliffs of Moher

Two hundred and fourteen metres high. Eight kilometres long. The most visited natural attraction in Ireland. The scale doesn't register from photos -- you have to stand at the edge, look straight down at the Atlantic hammering the cliff base, and feel the wind trying to push you back. On a clear day you can see the Aran Islands to the north and the mountains of Kerry to the south.

Cliffs of Moher
Nature Romantic Liscannor, Co. Clare
The visitor centre is built into the hillside. Inside: Atlantic Edge exhibition, two cafes, gift shop. O'Brien's Tower (1835) sits at the highest point with 360-degree views. Walk south from the visitor centre toward Hag's Head for fewer crowds and arguably more dramatic views.
March hours: 8 AM -- 7 PM daily. Adult admission €10 (online at cliffsofmoher.ie) or €12 at the gate. Children under 12 free. Includes parking, exhibition, and O'Brien's Tower. Allow 1--1.5 hours.
Weather-dependent
The cliffs are fully exposed to the Atlantic. In heavy rain or fog, you can't see anything -- the cliff edge vanishes into grey mist and you're paying €10 to stand in a cloud. Check conditions that morning before committing. Friday March 20 forecast: 12--13°C, cloudy but DRY, almost no wind (2 m/s). That's a good cliff day.

Doolin

A tiny village 15 minutes from the Cliffs that somehow became the spiritual home of traditional Irish music. Three pubs, all within walking distance, all hosting live trad sessions most nights -- even in March. Good for lunch before the drive south.

Gus O'Connor's Pub
Lunch Trad Music Fisher Street, Doolin
Established 1832. The most famous of Doolin's three music pubs. Nightly trad sessions. Also serves lunch and dinner. Un-amplified sessions -- the real thing.
Vegetarian Options
McGann's Pub
Lunch Trad Music Main Street, Doolin
Family-run since 1975. More intimate than Gus O'Connor's. Proper pub food -- stew with soda bread, fish and chips. Cozy stone fireplace.
Vegetarian Options

The drive south to Cork

From Doolin, the route to Cork goes inland through Ennis and Limerick, then south on the motorway. It's about 3.5 hours of driving, most of it on fast roads.

Doolin → Ennis
~30 minutes via R478/N85. Ennis is a charming market town if you need a quick stop or stretch. County town of Clare.
Ennis → Limerick
~40 minutes on the N18 dual carriageway. Fast, easy driving. The route bypasses the city centre.
Limerick → Cork
~1 hour 50 minutes on the N20/M20 motorway. Flat, fast, uneventful. You'll cross into County Cork before you know it.
Total: Doolin to Cork
~3 hours driving. Add stops and you're looking at 3.5--4 hours door to door.

Running late after the Cliffs?
If it's past 2:00 PM when you leave the Cliffs of Moher, skip Doolin and drive straight to Ennis. You can pick up food in Ennis instead. The priority is arriving in Cork before dark (sunset ~6:40 PM in late March). Every 30 minutes you linger adds 30 minutes of dark driving on unfamiliar roads.
If you left the Aran Islands Friday morning
The 8:15 AM ferry gets you back to Rossaveel by ~9:00 AM. Drive to Galway, grab coffee, and you're heading south by 9:30--10:00 AM. You can still fit in Poulnabrone and the Cliffs, but lunch in Doolin becomes tight. Consider grabbing food to go in Ennis instead of sitting down in Doolin.
