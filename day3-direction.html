Where the Trip Turns

You're back in Galway. The islands are behind you -- the stone walls, the Atlantic wind, the silence. Now comes the question that shapes the rest of your trip. You have a rental car, roughly three days, and a hard deadline: Dublin Airport Terminal 2 by 2:00 PM Tuesday, March 24th for your 4:45 PM Aer Lingus flight home. US preclearance means you need that extra time.

Every route below gets you to that flight. They just show you a different Ireland along the way.

Route A The Wild Atlantic Way South
Galway → The Burren → Cliffs of Moher → Doolin → Dingle → Killarney → Kenmare → Kinsale → Cork
This is the route travel writers rave about. Coastal cliffs dropping into the Atlantic, medieval fishing villages, the Dingle Peninsula, and Ireland's best food town (Kinsale). Three days of driving with stops at every turn. It's the most you can see in the time you have left.
What you gain
  • Cliffs of Moher
  • Dingle Peninsula
  • Ring of Kerry
  • Kinsale -- Ireland's food capital
  • The full Wild Atlantic Way experience
What you miss
  • Dublin entirely (no Kilmainham, no Kevin & Howlin -- unless you already went Day 1)
  • Tuesday logistics are tight
Logistics
Car return: Cork Airport (one-way rental, ~$124 for the trip). Then you need to get from Cork to Dublin Airport on Tuesday. Options: drive the full way back (~3 hrs, but then it's a round-trip rental, not one-way), Cork→Dublin Heuston train (2.5 hrs, ~€30--50), or fly Cork→Dublin (30 min, check Aer Lingus/Ryanair).
The tradeoff
You'll see the most dramatic coastline in Ireland. But you won't see Dublin again, and you need a plan for getting from Cork to Dublin Airport on Tuesday morning. That's either a 3-hour drive, a 2.5-hour train, or a short flight. Not impossible -- but it needs to be sorted in advance.

Cliffs, peninsulas, fishing villages, Kinsale. The showpiece route.

Route B Coastal Clare, Then Back to Dublin
Galway → The Burren → Cliffs of Moher → Doolin → back east through Ennis/Limerick → Dublin
You get the Cliffs of Moher and the Burren -- the two most iconic sights on the west coast -- then cut back east to finish the trip in Dublin. This gives you a last evening or morning in the city for Kilmainham, tweed shopping, or a proper Dublin pub session at Palace Bar.
What you gain
  • Cliffs of Moher
  • The Burren
  • A full Dublin send-off (Kilmainham, Kevin & Howlin, Palace Bar)
  • Simpler logistics on Tuesday
What you miss
  • Dingle Peninsula entirely
  • Ring of Kerry
  • Kinsale
  • Everything south of Clare
Logistics
Car return: Dublin Airport (round-trip rental, simpler and cheaper). You wake up Tuesday in Dublin, drive to the airport, done. No trains, no flights, no stress.
The tradeoff
You get the Cliffs AND Dublin. But you miss Dingle, the Ring of Kerry, and Kinsale entirely. If you'll come back to Ireland someday, those can wait. If this is the only trip, they can't.
Choose Route B: Clare + Dublin Return →
The Cliffs and the Burren, then a proper Dublin finish.

Route C The Midlands & Dublin
Galway → Strokestown (National Famine Museum) → Kilbeggan Distillery (if skipped on way out) → Dublin
If you care more about Irish political history than coastal scenery, this is your route. The Famine Museum at Strokestown Park is one of the most powerful historical sites in the country -- a landlord's estate with the original eviction records still in the archive. You skip every cliff and fishing village on the west coast, but you get depth instead of breadth.
What you gain
  • Strokestown Park & National Famine Museum
  • Kilbeggan Distillery (if you missed it)
  • Full day+ in Dublin for museums, shopping, pubs
  • Most relaxed pace, least driving
What you miss
  • Every cliff, peninsula, and fishing village on the west coast
  • Cliffs of Moher, Dingle, Ring of Kerry, Kinsale
Logistics
Car return: Dublin Airport (round-trip rental). Shortest total drive time. You could be in Dublin by Saturday afternoon with two full days in the city before your Tuesday flight.
The tradeoff
The historical and political route. You trade coastal drama for historical weight. The Famine Museum alone justifies this path if Irish history matters to you. And you'll have plenty of time in Dublin to see Kilmainham, browse the tweed shops, and sit in a proper pub without checking the clock.

Famine Museum, whiskey, and a deep Dublin finish.

Route D Slow Galway & Connemara
Stay another night in Galway → day trip to Connemara (Rosmuc, Clifden, Sky Road) → head east to Dublin
The most relaxed option. Stay another night in Galway and spend a day in Connemara -- the wild, boggy, Irish-speaking heartland west of the city. Visit Pearse's Cottage in Rosmuc (the cottage where Patrick Pearse retreated to learn Irish before the 1916 Rising -- it opens March 17th for the season), drive the Sky Road near Clifden, walk in Connemara National Park. Then head east to Dublin at your own pace.
What you gain
  • Connemara -- deep Gaeltacht immersion
  • Pearse's Cottage (just opened for the season)
  • Sky Road & Connemara National Park
  • Most relaxed pace of any route
  • Time in Dublin on the way back
What you miss
  • The entire southern coast
  • Cliffs of Moher, Dingle, Kinsale
  • The Burren
Logistics
Car return: Dublin Airport (round-trip rental). Easy drive east when you're ready. You set the pace.
The tradeoff
Deep Gaeltacht immersion and wild landscape, but you skip the southern coast entirely. This is the right call if you're exhausted from the islands and want to slow down rather than pile on more driving. Connemara is beautiful and empty in a way that rewards stillness over speed.

Before You Decide

Route A: Cork-to-Dublin logistics
If you're doing Route A (Wild Atlantic Way to Cork), you need to figure out how to get from Cork to Dublin Airport on Tuesday. Three options: drive the car all the way back (~3 hrs, but then it's a round-trip rental, not one-way), train Cork→Dublin Heuston (2.5 hrs, ~€30--50, then taxi to the airport), or fly Cork→Dublin (~30 min, check Aer Lingus/Ryanair -- often €30--60 if booked early). The train is the most reliable; the flight is the fastest. Don't leave this for the last day.
Book tonight's accommodation now
If you haven't booked tonight's accommodation yet, do it now. Doolin and Dingle B&Bs fill up even in March -- they're small towns with limited beds. Galway is easier but still books fast on weekends.
Weather window
The forecast is dry through Sunday, with rain arriving Tuesday. Routes A and B -- where you're on exposed coastal roads and cliff walks -- are dramatically better in dry weather. If rain moves in earlier than expected, the midlands route (C) is more comfortable, and Connemara (D) is moody but manageable.
• • •

How to Choose

If this is your only trip to Ireland ever, do Route A. You'll see the most. The logistics are worth it for the Cliffs of Moher, Dingle, and Kinsale in one sweep.
If you'll come back someday, Route B or C lets you save Kerry and Cork for next time and enjoy Dublin properly on this trip. You can always do the full southern loop on a return visit.
If you're feeling exhausted from the islands and want to slow down, Route D. Connemara rewards stillness, and you won't spend your remaining days in the car.
If Irish political history matters more to you than scenery, Route C. Strokestown's Famine Museum plus Kilmainham Gaol in Dublin is the most historically powerful two-day combination in Ireland.
