This route trades the wild west coast for time. Instead of racing from Galway to the Cliffs to Kerry to Cork in a jet-lagged blur, you take the direct motorway south and arrive in Cork/Kinsale by Wednesday evening or Thursday morning. That gives you three or four full days in the Cork area instead of two. Less driving, more living. The best anniversary pace.

You still get Dublin's best sights on arrival day, and you can break the drive with some of Ireland's most striking medieval sites. You just don't try to see the entire country in a week.

Wednesday: Dublin Morning + Drive South

You land at 4:20 AM, pick up the rental car, and have a full morning in Dublin before heading south after lunch. The M7/M8 motorway to Cork is fast and straightforward -- wide, well-signed, mostly straight. You'll arrive in Cork or Kinsale by evening, or you can break the drive in Kilkenny and arrive Thursday morning.

Early Breakfast (5:30--8 AM)

You'll clear customs and get the car by 5:30--6:00 AM. The airport is 30 minutes from Dublin city centre. Most cafes don't open until 8, so your early options are limited.

Keogh's Cafe
Breakfast 1--2 Trinity Street, Dublin 2
Opens at 6:30 AM on weekdays -- one of the earliest independent cafes in the city. Right next to Trinity College. Good coffee, full Irish breakfast, and the kind of no-fuss spot where you can stare at the wall for twenty minutes while your brain catches up with your body.
Mon--Sat 6:30 AM -- 6:30 PM
Vegetarian options available
Cornucopia
Breakfast / Lunch 19/20 Wicklow Street, Dublin 2
Fully vegetarian and vegan since 1986. Opens at 8 AM on weekdays, so this works if you take it slow and arrive in the city around 7:30. Self-service counter with hot dishes, soups, pastries -- all made fresh. Two floors, relaxed atmosphere. Savannah would love this place.
Wed: 8:00 AM -- 7:00 PM
100% Vegetarian & Vegan

Dublin Highlights (9 AM -- 12:30 PM)

You have about three and a half hours. You won't do all of these, but you can fit two or three depending on how deep you go.

Kilmainham Gaol
History Inchicore Road, Kilmainham, Dublin 8
The single most powerful political history site in Ireland. This is where the leaders of the 1916 Rising were held and executed -- Pearse, Connolly, Plunkett (who married Grace Gifford in the chapel hours before his execution). The guided tour walks through the cells, the yards, and the execution courtyard. Devastating and unforgettable.
Access is by guided tour only. Tours take about 60 minutes, plus 30 minutes for the museum. Tickets are €8 per adult.
Generally open from 9:30 AM. Tours run throughout the day.
Tickets may be sold out
Kilmainham releases tickets exactly 28 days in advance. For Wednesday March 18, those tickets dropped on February 18 and likely sold out within hours. But: cancellation tickets are released online each morning between 9:15 and 9:30 AM. Refresh kilmainhamgaolmuseum.ie at 9:15 sharp. Only buy from the official OPW site -- third-party tickets aren't valid.
If Kilmainham is sold out
National Museum of Ireland -- Collins Barracks is free, 15 minutes from Kilmainham, and houses the Proclaiming a Republic: The 1916 Rising exhibition. It covers the same ground through personal belongings, uniforms, bullets, and first-aid kits from rebels, British soldiers, and civilians. Genuinely excellent, and you can walk in without a ticket. Hours: Tue--Sat 10 AM -- 5 PM. Benburb Street, Dublin 7.
Kevin & Howlin
Tweed 31 Nassau Street, Dublin 2
The gold standard for Donegal tweed in Ireland. Founded in 1936, specializing exclusively in handwoven Donegal tweed since 1973. Jackets, caps, scarves -- heritage shop, not a tourist trap. Old-world service with incredibly knowledgeable staff who'll walk you through different weights and weaves. Opposite Trinity College.
Mon--Sat 9:30 AM -- 5:30 PM
GPO / O'Connell Street
History O'Connell Street, Dublin 1
The General Post Office was rebel headquarters during the 1916 Rising -- where Pearse read the Proclamation of the Republic from the front steps. The bullet marks in the columns are original. The museum inside is interactive and well-produced. Allow about 90 minutes if you go in, or just walk past and see the facade.
Mon--Sat 10:00 AM -- 5:00 PM. Closed Sundays.
Trinity College
Culture College Green, Dublin 2
Walk the grounds for free -- the cobblestone front square is beautiful even without going inside any buildings. The Book of Kells and the Long Room are worth the visit if you have 90 minutes to spare (tickets €18--25, book at visittrinity.ie), but if time is tight, just walk through the grounds on your way between Kevin & Howlin and lunch.
Grounds open from early morning. Book of Kells: Wed 9:30 AM -- 5:00 PM.

Lunch Before Driving

Eat before you drive. Hunger plus jet lag plus left-side driving is a bad combination.

Creative, beautifully plated dishes -- proper fine dining, not a hippie buffet. Perfect for Savannah. Reserve via OpenTable or call +353 1 672 4534.

Wednesday Afternoon: The Drive South

Leave Dublin by early afternoon, take the M7/M8 motorway south. It's about 2.5--3 hours to Cork without stops. The motorway is fast and forgiving -- wide, well-signed, mostly straight. This is where you choose your pace.

◆ EN ROUTE: PICK YOUR STOPS
Option A: Drive Straight to Cork/Kinsale
Leave Dublin by 1:30 PM, arrive Cork by 4:30--5:00 PM, Kinsale by 5:30. You're settled in for dinner. Maximum Cork time starts now.
~2.5--3 hr drive Arrive Wed evening 4 full days in Cork area
Option B: Stop at Rock of Cashel
45--60 minutes off the motorway for one of Ireland's most iconic sights. Then continue to Cork, arriving by early evening.
Adds ~1 hr Right off the M8 Arrive Cork ~6:00 PM
Option C: Overnight in Kilkenny
Take the M9 fork to Kilkenny (about 1.5 hrs from Dublin). Explore the Medieval Mile, have dinner in a proper Irish town, sleep there. Drive to Cork Thursday morning -- only 1.5 hours. You still get 3 full days in the Cork area.
~1.5 hr to Kilkenny Medieval city Arrive Cork Thu AM

Rock of Cashel

Rock of Cashel
Heritage Site Cashel, Co. Tipperary -- ~1.5 hrs from Dublin on the M8
One of Ireland's most iconic silhouettes. A cluster of 12th-century ruins on a dramatic hilltop -- cathedral, round tower, Romanesque chapel, all of it exposed to the wind on a limestone outcrop rising from the Tipperary plain. This was the seat of the Kings of Munster for centuries before it became an ecclesiastical site. You can see it from the motorway. Pull off, walk up, spend an hour, get back on the road.
Open 9 AM -- 5 PM in March — €8 — allow 45--60 min

Cahir Castle

Cahir Castle
Castle Cahir, Co. Tipperary -- ~20 min north of Cashel
One of the largest and best-preserved medieval castles in Ireland, sitting on a rock island in the River Suir. Curtain walls, keep, great hall -- all intact. Cromwell besieged it in 1599 and barely dented it. If you're already stopping at Cashel, Cahir is a quick add-on just up the road. Smaller but atmospheric.
Open daily — €5 — allow ~45 min

Kilkenny (Overnight Option)

Kilkenny
Medieval City Co. Kilkenny -- ~1.5 hrs from Dublin, ~1.5 hrs from Cork
The Medieval Mile connects Kilkenny Castle (13th century, free grounds) to St. Canice's Cathedral through narrow lanes lined with craft shops, pubs, and the Smithwick's brewery. It's one of the most walkable towns in Ireland and feels more like a small European city than a county town. A proper overnight gives you time to explore instead of rushing through.
Kilkenny Castle: open daily, €8 — St. Canice's Cathedral: €6
The Kilkenny option
If you're fading from jet lag by mid-afternoon (you will be -- you've been up since 4 AM Irish time), Kilkenny is a smart call. Stop driving at a reasonable hour, explore a beautiful medieval town, have a relaxed dinner, sleep. Then drive to Cork on Thursday morning feeling human. You trade one Cork evening for a much better first day.

Thursday: Arrive Cork (or Bonus Day)

This is where the Slow South route pays off. Depending on what you chose Wednesday, you're either arriving fresh from Kilkenny or already waking up in Cork with a full bonus day ahead of you.

If You Overnighted in Kilkenny

Morning
Walk the Medieval Mile one more time. Morning light on the castle grounds. Breakfast at a local cafe.
Late Morning
Drive to Cork -- only 1.5 hours on the M8. Straight shot, no narrow roads.
Early Afternoon
Arrive in Kinsale or Cork City. Check into your B&B, settle in. Your weekend base starts now.
Afternoon
First wander around Kinsale's harbor and colorful streets, or hit the English Market in Cork City (Mon--Sat, 8 AM -- 6 PM).
Evening
First Cork-area dinner. You've arrived.

If You Arrived Cork on Wednesday Night

Full bonus day. This is the luxury of the Slow South route. Some options:

Cobh
Heritage Town 20 min from Cork City
The last port of call for the Titanic. The Cobh Heritage Centre tells the story of Irish emigration -- millions left from this harbor and never came back. St. Colman's Cathedral sits on the hill above the town, visible from everywhere. The harbor walk is deeply atmospheric. A genuinely moving half-day trip.
Heritage Centre: daily 9:30 AM -- 5:00 PM. Adults ~€10.
West Cork Coast
Scenic Drive Michael Collins Country
Drive the West Cork coast through Timoleague, Clonakilty, and toward Bantry Bay. Timoleague Friary is a 13th-century Franciscan ruin on the banks of the Argideen River -- the largest medieval ruin in West Cork, free entry, always accessible. Michael Collins House in Clonakilty covers the War of Independence through guided tours and artifacts from Collins' home town.
Michael Collins House: Tue--Sat 10 AM -- 5 PM. Timoleague Friary: free, daylight hours.
Early Kinsale Exploration
Anniversary Pace
Get a head start on your weekend base. Walk to Charles Fort via the Scilly Walk (35 min along the harbor, fort is €5 entry). Browse Kinsale's galleries and shops. Have lunch at Fishy Fishy on Crowley's Quay. No schedule, no driving, just settling in.

Friday: Another Full Day

This is the day that doesn't exist on the west coast route. If you'd gone through Galway and Kerry, you'd be driving to Cork right now. Instead, you're waking up with nowhere to be.

Options, all of them optional:

Cork City
Food Capital
Hit the English Market (Mon--Sat, 8 AM -- 6 PM) -- one of Europe's oldest covered food markets, Cork's beating heart. Lunch at the Farmgate Cafe on the mezzanine level overlooking the stalls. Climb Shandon Bells & Tower and ring the bells yourself over Cork's rooftops (€5). Walk the River Lee through the UCC campus.
Farmgate Cafe: good vegetarian options
Blarney Castle
Castle & Gardens 15 min from Cork City
Yes, the Blarney Stone is touristy. But the castle grounds and gardens are genuinely beautiful, and the castle itself is a proper medieval tower house with interesting rooms to explore. If you're curious, go -- you're not in a rush. If it doesn't appeal, skip it without guilt.
Slow Kinsale Wandering
Anniversary Pace
Sleep in. Breakfast at The Lemon Leaf Cafe on Main Street. Walk the harbor. Browse galleries. Have a long lunch at The Black Pig with wine and sharing plates. Nap. Dinner at Bastion (Michelin star, book ahead) or Cafe Paradiso in Cork City (Ireland's most celebrated vegetarian restaurant). This is the luxury of this route: no rushing, no "we have to leave by 9 AM."
Lemon Leaf, Black Pig, Cafe Paradiso: all excellent veg options
This is the point
The whole argument for the Slow South is right here: a Friday with no obligations. You've been in Ireland for two days, you're over the worst of the jet lag, and you have three more full days in this area. No one is driving four hours today. No one is catching a ferry. You're just here.

The Honest Tradeoffs

What you gain
  • 1--2 extra full days in the Cork/Kinsale area -- more meals, more wandering, more anniversary pace
  • Kilmainham Gaol on Day 1 -- the most important political history site in Ireland, which you'd miss entirely on the west coast route
  • Kevin & Howlin and the Dublin tweed shops -- your only chance if you're doing a one-way rental to Cork
  • Less driving overall -- the M7/M8 is fast motorway, not narrow coastal roads
  • Less jet-lag pressure -- you're not navigating mountain passes on three hours of sleep
  • Time for Cobh, West Cork, or Blarney -- day trips that don't exist when you're rushing through Kerry
What you miss
  • Galway -- the bohemian west coast city, the pubs, the buskers on Shop Street
  • Aran Islands -- Inis Mor, the stone forts, the sweater shops, the ferry crossing
  • Cliffs of Moher -- the most dramatic coastal scenery in Ireland
  • Kerry and Dingle -- the Ring of Kerry, Skellig Michael views, Dingle's fishing-village charm
  • The wild west coast in general -- you can't get these on another trip easily, but you also can't get back the energy you'd spend chasing them jet-lagged
The real question
Do you want to see Ireland or feel Ireland? The west coast route is about seeing as much as possible. The Slow South is about settling into a place and letting it come to you. Neither is wrong. But for an anniversary trip after a red-eye, slowing down might be the move that makes the whole week feel like a holiday instead of a tour.
